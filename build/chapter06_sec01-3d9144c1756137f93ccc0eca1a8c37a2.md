# 6.1 Derivatives of Elementary Functions

In chapter 5 we computed derivatives from the limit definition, expanding and
cancelling step by step. For the parabolic marble track that calculation took
the better part of a page of algebra. That approach is rigorous and
illuminating, but repeating it from scratch every time we encounter a new
function would make practical work impossibly slow. The solution is to carry
out the derivation once for each family of elementary functions and collect the
results in a reference table. In this section we build that table for the
functions introduced in chapters 3 and 4: power functions, the exponential
function, the natural logarithm, and the trigonometric functions.

```{admonition} Learning goals
:class: attention
* [ ] You know the derivative of **power functions**: $(x^n)' = n\,x^{n-1}$
  for $n \in \mathbb{R}$.
* [ ] You know the derivatives of the **exponential function** $(e^x)' = e^x$
  and the **natural logarithm** $(\ln x)' = \dfrac{1}{x}$.
* [ ] You know the derivatives of the **trigonometric functions**
  $(\sin x)' = \cos x$, $(\cos x)' = -\sin x$, and
  $(\tan x)' = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x}$.
```

## What does the marble track already tell us?

In section 5.3 we applied the limit definition to the parabolic track height
$f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6$ at a general point $x$ and found

\begin{equation*}
f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6.
\end{equation*}

Looking at how each term transformed, a pattern emerges. The term $0.02\,x^2$
became $0.04\,x$: the exponent $2$ descended as a multiplicative factor and
the exponent decreased by one. The term $0.6\,x = 0.6\,x^1$ became $0.6$:
the same pattern with exponent $1$. The constant $6 = 6\,x^0$ vanished:
multiplying by the exponent $0$ kills the term. *Is this a general rule, or
a coincidence of this particular function?*

## What is the power rule?

It is a general rule. To see why, we apply the limit definition to
$f(x) = x^n$ for a positive integer $n$. When we expand $(x + h)^n$, the
result is a sum that starts with $x^n$, followed by a term $n\,x^{n-1}\,h$,
and then terms that all contain $h^2$ or higher powers of $h$:

\begin{equation*}
(x + h)^n = x^n + n\,x^{n-1}\,h + (\text{terms with } h^2, h^3, \ldots, h^n).
\end{equation*}

Subtracting $x^n$, dividing by $h$, and letting $h \to 0$, every term
containing a remaining factor of $h$ vanishes and we are left with

\begin{equation*}
(x^n)' = n\,x^{n-1}.
\end{equation*}

```{admonition} What is ... the power rule?
:class: note
For any real exponent $n \in \mathbb{R}$:

\begin{equation*}
\bigl(x^n\bigr)' = n\,x^{n-1}.
\end{equation*}
```

The rule extends to all real exponents, not only positive integers. For
$n = -1$ we obtain $(x^{-1})' = -x^{-2}$, and for $n = \tfrac{1}{2}$ we
obtain $(\sqrt{x}\,)' = \tfrac{1}{2}\,x^{-1/2}$. The derivation for
non-integer exponents requires a short additional argument using the chain
rule, which we return to in section 6.4.

Returning to our marble track: applying the power rule to each term of
$f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6$ recovers $f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6$
immediately. The same result that required a full page in section 5.3 now
follows in a single line. The rules for combining terms systematically are
formalised in section 6.2.

## What are the derivatives of the exponential function and the natural logarithm?

In section 4.1 we introduced Euler's number through its defining property:
$e^x$ is the only exponential function whose rate of change at every point
equals its own value at that point. Translated directly into derivative
notation, this reads

\begin{equation*}
(e^x)' = e^x.
\end{equation*}

No limit calculation is required. The equation is not a result we derive from
scratch; it is a direct translation of what makes $e$ special in the first
place.

## What are the derivatives of the trigonometric functions?

For the sine function, expanding $\sin(x + h)$ with the addition theorem from
section 4.5 and applying the standard limits $\lim_{h \to 0}\tfrac{\sin h}{h} = 1$
and $\lim_{h \to 0}\tfrac{\cos h - 1}{h} = 0$ gives

\begin{equation*}
(\sin x)' = \cos x.
\end{equation*}

The same procedure applied to cosine yields

\begin{equation*}
(\cos x)' = -\sin x.
\end{equation*}

The negative sign has a direct geometric meaning: wherever sine is rising,
cosine is falling. On the unit circle the two functions are always a quarter
period out of phase, and differentiation reflects this relationship exactly.

The derivative of the tangent follows from the quotient rule, which we
develop in section 6.3. The result is

\begin{equation*}
(\tan x)' = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x},
\end{equation*}

valid wherever $\cos x \neq 0$.

These are not only abstract formulas. The position of a mass in a vibrating
mechanical system is often a product of an exponential decay and a
trigonometric function. Computing the velocity of that mass requires
differentiating exactly this kind of expression, and we will work through it
in detail starting from section 6.3.

## A reference table for elementary derivatives

The results of this section are most useful when collected in a single place.
The following table is the practical starting point for every differentiation
problem in the rest of this chapter.

| Function $f(x)$ | Derivative $f'(x)$ | Condition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $x^n$ | $n\,x^{n-1}$ | $n \in \mathbb{R}$ |
| $e^x$ | $e^x$ | |
| $\ln x$ | $\dfrac{1}{x}$ | $x > 0$ |
| $\sin x$ | $\cos x$ | |
| $\cos x$ | $-\sin x$ | |
| $\tan x$ | $\dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x}$ | $\cos x \neq 0$ |

## Summary and outlook

We have established the derivatives of all the elementary function families
from chapters 3 and 4. The power rule unifies the polynomial results from
chapter 5: every monomial $x^n$ differentiates by bringing the exponent down
as a factor and reducing it by one, recovering in a single step the derivative
of the marble track height that cost a full page of algebra in section 5.3.
The exponential function $e^x$ is its own derivative, a property that singles
out Euler's number as the natural base for calculus. Sine and cosine
differentiate into each other with a sign change that reflects their phase
relationship on the unit circle. The derivatives of the tangent and the
natural logarithm are stated here for the reference table and derived
properly in sections 6.3 and 6.5 respectively. With this table in hand, the
next question is how to combine results: the following section introduces the
rules for differentiating sums and scalar multiples of functions, completing
the toolkit for any linear combination of elementary functions.
