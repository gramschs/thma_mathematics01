# 7.3 Convexity and Concavity

In section 5.3 we saw that the sign of the first derivative tells us whether a
function is increasing or decreasing: $f'(x) > 0$ means the graph rises,
$f'(x) < 0$ means it falls. The second derivative provides one more layer of
information. It tells us not just whether the function is rising or falling,
but whether the rate of change is itself growing or shrinking. Geometrically,
this is the difference between a curve that bends upward, like the base of a
valley, and a curve that bends downward, like the crown of a hill. This
bending behavior affects how the graph looks and how its tangent lines sit
relative to the curve.

```{admonition} Learning goals
:class: attention

* [ ] You know the definitions of **convexity** (concave up) and **concavity** (concave down) of a function on an interval.
* [ ] You can determine intervals of convexity and concavity from the sign of the second derivative: $f''(x) > 0$ implies convex, $f''(x) < 0$ implies concave.

* [ ] You understand the geometric interpretation of the second derivative's sign in terms of the curvature of the graph and the behavior of the tangent line.

```

## What does the sign of the second derivative reveal on the track?

We return to the roller coaster height profile from section 7.1:

\begin{equation*}
h(x) = \frac{1}{30}\,x^2 - \frac{1}{2700}\,x^3, \quad x \in [0, 60],
\end{equation*}

with second derivative $h''(x) = \dfrac{30 - x}{450}$. This expression is
positive for $x < 30$, zero at $x = 30$, and negative for $x > 30$.

*What does this sign change mean for the shape of the track?*

On the lower half of the lift hill, $x \in [0, 30]$, the second derivative is
positive. The slope $h'(x) = \frac{x(60-x)}{900}$ is therefore increasing: it
starts at zero at the boarding platform and grows steadily, reaching its
maximum value of $1$ at $x = 30~\text{m}$. If we draw the tangent line at
successive positions along this stretch, each new tangent is steeper than the
one before. Looking at the profile from the side, the track curves upward,
bending toward the sky, like the inside of a bowl.

On the upper half, $x \in [30, 60]$, the second derivative is negative. The
slope $h'(x)$ is now decreasing: from its peak of $1$ at $x = 30$ it falls
back to zero at the summit. Each tangent line is less steep than its
predecessor. The track curves downward, bending away from the sky, like the
outside of a dome.

The table below records the slope and second derivative at five positions along
the lift hill, making the contrast explicit.

| Position $x$ [m] | $h'(x)$ | $h''(x)$ | Shape of track |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $0$ | $0$ | $\tfrac{1}{15} > 0$ | begins to curve upward |
| $15$ | $\tfrac{3}{4}$ | $\tfrac{1}{30} > 0$ | curving upward, rising |
| $30$ | $1$ | $0$ | steepest point, curvature changes |
| $45$ | $\tfrac{3}{4}$ | $-\tfrac{1}{30} < 0$ | curving downward, still rising |
| $60$ | $0$ | $-\tfrac{1}{15} < 0$ | levels off at the summit |

The contrast between $x = 15$ and $x = 45$ is particularly instructive. At
both positions the slope is $\frac{3}{4}$: the track rises at exactly the same
gradient. Yet the bending character is opposite. At $x = 15$ the gradient is
still growing, and the tangent line lies below the track nearby. At $x = 45$
the gradient is diminishing, and the tangent line lies above the track nearby.

## What are convexity and concavity?

The two shapes we have described have precise names. A function whose slope is
increasing, so that the graph bends upward, is called **convex** on that
interval, or equivalently concave up. A function whose slope is decreasing,
so that the graph bends downward, is called **concave** on that interval, or
equivalently concave down.

```{admonition} What is ... convexity and concavity?
:class: note
Let $f$ be twice differentiable on an open interval $I$.

$f$ is **convex** (concave up) on $I$ if $f''(x) > 0$ for all $x \in I$. The
slope is increasing, the graph bends upward, and every tangent line lies
below the graph on that interval.

$f$ is **concave** (concave down) on $I$ if $f''(x) < 0$ for all $x \in I$.
The slope is decreasing, the graph bends downward, and every tangent line
lies above the graph on that interval.

In this course we use the word \emph{convex} for what many textbooks call
“concave up”, and \emph{concave} for what many textbooks call “concave down”.
We will mainly talk about the simple case where the second derivative exists
and is strictly positive or strictly negative on the interval.
```

The tangent-line characterisation in the definition deserves attention. For a
convex function the tangent at any point underestimates the function value at
nearby points: the true curve is always above the straight-line approximation.
For a concave function the tangent overestimates: the true curve is below.
This connects directly to the linear approximation formula
$f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0)$ developed in section 5.5. On a
convex interval the formula underestimates the true value, and on a concave
interval it overestimates it.

For the roller coaster lift hill we summarise: $h$ is convex on $[0, 30]$ and
concave on $[30, 60]$. The transition occurs at $x = 30~\text{m}$, the point
of steepest ascent.

## Where does convexity matter in engineering?

Convexity and concavity appear throughout structural and civil engineering
wherever the shape of a loaded member or a geometric path must be described
precisely.

In beam theory, the deflection of a simply supported beam under load satisfies
the relation $EI\,y'' = M(x)$, where $E$ is the modulus of elasticity,
$I$ is the second moment of area, and $M(x)$ is the bending moment at
cross-section $x$. The sign of $y''$ at each section identifies whether the
beam is sagging (bending downwards in the middle, convex upward, $y'' > 0$)
or hogging (bending upwards in the middle, concave, $y'' < 0$). These two
regimes produce tension in opposite fibres of the cross-section, and the
reinforcement layout in a concrete beam is designed with this distinction in
mind.

In road and railway design, the vertical alignment alternates between convex
sections at summits and concave sections in valleys. The curvature at each
point, directly proportional to $|h''|$, determines the minimum sight distance
on a crest curve and the dynamic wheel load on a vehicle passing through a sag
curve at speed. Both quantities are checked against design standards before a
vertical profile is approved.

## Summary and outlook

We have given the sign of the second derivative a geometric interpretation. A
positive second derivative means the slope is growing and the graph bends
upward: the function is convex. A negative second derivative means the slope
is shrinking and the graph bends downward: the function is concave. For the
roller coaster height profile the lower half of the lift hill is convex and
the upper half is concave.

At $x = 30~\text{m}$, where $h'' = 0$, the curvature character reverses. This
transition is not just a zero of the second derivative: it marks a point where
the function changes from one geometric type to another. Such a point has its
own name, and confirming that it is genuine requires a check that goes beyond
simply setting $h'' = 0$. That is the subject of the next section.
