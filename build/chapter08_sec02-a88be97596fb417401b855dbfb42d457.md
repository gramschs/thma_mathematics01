# 8.2 Curve Sketching

We now have all the tools needed to draw a qualitatively accurate graph of
any differentiable function without plotting individual points. The zeros of
$f$ anchor the curve to the axis. The sign of $f'$ marks the intervals of
increase and decrease identified in chapter 5, and its zeros locate the
critical points classified in section 8.1. The sign of $f''$ marks the convex
and concave intervals from section 7.3, and its zeros locate the inflection
points of section 7.4. Combining all this information into a single coherent
sketch is the technique of **curve sketching**, and it brings together nearly
everything we have developed since chapter 5.

```{admonition} Learning goals
:class: attention
* [ ] You can carry out a systematic **curve sketching** procedure: determining the domain, zeros, $y$-intercept, monotonicity intervals, local extrema, convexity intervals, and inflection points.
* [ ] You can produce a qualitatively correct sketch of a differentiable function using only the information obtained from $f$, $f'$, and $f''$.
* [ ] You understand the relationships between the graphs of $f$, $f'$, and $f''$, and you can read information about one from the graph of another.
```

## What is the curve sketching procedure?

Every curve sketch follows the same sequence of steps, each building on the
last:

1. Determine the domain and note any restrictions, asymptotes, or symmetries.
2. Find the $y$-intercept $f(0)$ and the zeros of $f$.
3. Compute $f'$, find the critical points where $f' = 0$, and determine the
   sign of $f'$ on each subinterval.
4. Classify each critical point as a local maximum or minimum using the
   second derivative test from section 8.1.
5. Compute $f''$, find the inflection candidates where $f'' = 0$, and verify
   sign changes to confirm each one.
6. Evaluate $f$ at all critical points, inflection points, and domain
   endpoints.
7. Combine all findings into a coherent sketch.

We now carry out this procedure for a concrete engineering function, then
examine what the three graphs $f$, $f'$, and $f''$ reveal about each other.

## How do we sketch the speed profile of a test sled?

A motorised sled on a straight test track performs a controlled braking
sequence. Starting from rest, the sled accelerates to a test speed, then
brakes back to a complete stop before re-accelerating. The speed of the sled,
in m/s, as a function of time $t$ in seconds is modelled by

\begin{equation*}
v(t) = t(t - 3)^2, \quad t \in [0, 4].
\end{equation*}

We sketch the graph of $v$ by following the seven-step procedure above.

**Step 1: Domain and structure.** The domain is the closed interval $[0, 4]$.
The function is a polynomial of degree three, continuous and differentiable
everywhere on this interval.

**Step 2: Intercepts and zeros.** The $y$-intercept is $v(0) = 0$. The
factored form $t(t - 3)^2$ reveals the zeros immediately: $t = 0$ (a simple
zero, the curve crosses the axis) and $t = 3$ (a double zero, the curve
touches the axis but does not cross it). A double zero at $t = 3$ means the
sled momentarily stops and then resumes motion in the same direction.

**Step 3: First derivative and monotonicity.** Expanding and differentiating:

\begin{equation*}
v(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t, \quad
v'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t - 1)(t - 3).
\end{equation*}

The critical points are $t = 1$ and $t = 3$. Checking the sign of $v'$:

On $(0, 1)$: $v'(0.5) = 3(-0.5)(-2.5) = 3.75 > 0$. Speed increasing.

On $(1, 3)$: $v'(2) = 3(1)(-1) = -3 < 0$. Speed decreasing.

On $(3, 4)$: $v'(3.5) = 3(2.5)(0.5) = 3.75 > 0$. Speed increasing again.

**Step 4: Classification of critical points.** The second derivative is
$v''(t) = 6t - 12$. Evaluating at each critical point:

\begin{equation*}
v''(1) = 6 - 12 = -6 < 0 \implies \text{local maximum at } t = 1.
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
v''(3) = 18 - 12 = 6 > 0 \implies \text{local minimum at } t = 3.
\end{equation*}

**Step 5: Inflection point.** Setting $v''(t) = 0$ gives $6t - 12 = 0$,
so $t = 2$. Checking the sign change: $v''(1.5) = 9 - 12 = -3 < 0$ and
$v''(2.5) = 15 - 12 = 3 > 0$. The sign reverses at $t = 2$: confirmed
inflection point.

**Step 6: Key values.** We collect the function values at all special
points:

| $t$ [s] | $v(t)$ [m/s] | Character |
| --- | --- | --- |
| $0$ | $0$ | left endpoint |
| $1$ | $4$ | local maximum |
| $2$ | $2$ | inflection point |
| $3$ | $0$ | local minimum, double zero |
| $4$ | $4$ | right endpoint |

At $t = 1$ and $t = 4$ the speed is $4~\text{m/s}$. These tie as the
global maximum of $v$ on $[0, 4]$. The global minimum is $0~\text{m/s}$,
achieved at $t = 0$ and $t = 3$.

**Step 7: The sketch.** With all six pieces of information in hand, we can
assemble the graph. For $t \in [0, 2]$ the second derivative $v'' = 6t - 12$
is negative, so the curve is concave (bends downward). For $t \in [2, 4]$
the second derivative is positive, so the curve is convex (bends upward).

The curve starts at the origin, rises with downward curvature to the local
maximum at $(1,\, 4)$, continues descending through the inflection at
$(2,\, 2)$ where the curvature reverses, then arcs upward to the double zero
at $(3,\, 0)$ where it just touches the axis before rising again to the
endpoint at $(4,\, 4)$.

The double zero at $t = 3$ is clearly visible: the factor $(t - 3)^2$
ensures the curve is tangent to the $t$-axis there, touching it but not
crossing it. This corresponds physically to the sled decelerating smoothly
to a stop and then re-accelerating without reversing direction.

## How do the graphs of $v$, $v'$, and $v''$ relate to each other?

The three functions $v$, $v'$, and $v''$ are not independent: each is the
derivative of the one above it. Every feature of $v$ is encoded in the sign
or zero of $v'$ and $v''$, and reading across the three graphs fluently is
one of the core skills this section develops.

Where $v$ has a local maximum, $v'$ crosses zero from positive to negative.
At $t = 1$, the graph of $v'$ descends through zero. Where $v$ has a local
minimum, $v'$ crosses zero from negative to positive. At $t = 3$, $v'$
ascends through zero. The zeros of $v'$ are exactly the local extrema of $v$.

Where $v$ has an inflection point, $v'$ has a local extremum. The inflection
of $v$ at $t = 2$ corresponds to a local minimum of $v'$: $v'(2) = 3(1)(-1) = -3$,
the most negative value of $v'$ on the interval. This is consistent with the
geometric interpretation: at the inflection the curve transitions from
concave to convex, which means the slope, already negative and decreasing,
reaches its most negative point and then begins to recover.

The graph of $v'' = 6t - 12$ is a straight line that is negative to the
left of $t = 2$ and positive to the right. It crosses zero at the inflection
point of $v$, is negative at the local maximum $t = 1$ (confirming the
downward curvature there), and positive at the local minimum $t = 3$
(confirming the upward curvature). All three graphs tell the same story from
three different vantage points.

## Where does curve sketching appear in engineering?

The ability to read qualitative behaviour directly from derivatives is
valuable whenever a function is known in formula form and the engineer needs
to understand its structure quickly, without numerical simulation.

In control engineering, the step response of a system is a function of time
whose zeros (crossing times), local extrema (overshoot values), and
inflection points (moment of maximum rate of rise) each have direct
engineering significance. An engineer sketches the response curve from the
transfer function in order to check whether the system will produce
acceptable behaviour before committing to a detailed simulation. In
thermodynamics, the efficiency of a cycle as a function of one operating
parameter can be sketched to identify the optimal operating region and the
sensitivity of the efficiency to changes away from the optimum.

## Summary and outlook

We have assembled the full curve sketching procedure into a seven-step
sequence and applied it to the speed profile $v(t) = t(t - 3)^2$. The
sketch required no numerical plotting: the zeros, critical points, inflection
point, and curvature sign were all determined from $v'$ and $v''$ alone.
The three-panel view of $v$, $v'$, and $v''$ showed how each feature of the
curve is encoded in the sign or zero of its derivatives.

Curve sketching describes a function that is already given to us. In
engineering the more common task is the reverse: we have a design goal and
must find the variable values that achieve the best outcome. Setting up and
solving such problems is the subject of the next section.
