3.5 Quadratic Functions

Among all polynomials, the quadratic function of degree 2 deserves its own section. Its graph is a parabola, a curve that appears throughout engineering in contexts ranging from projectile trajectories to the cross-sections of parabolic reflectors. More importantly for us, the parabola has a lowest or highest point, called the vertex, that will become the central object of study when we reach differential calculus. In this section we develop all the tools needed to work with quadratic functions confidently.

Learning goals¶

Learning goals

  • You know the standard form f(x)=ax2+bx+cf(x) = ax^2 + bx + cf(x)=ax2+bx+c and the vertex form f(x)=a(x−xV)2+yVf(x) = a(x - x_V)^2 + y_Vf(x)=a(x−xV​)2+yV​ of a quadratic function and can convert between them.

  • You can identify the vertex V=(xV,yV)V = (x_V, y_V)V=(xV​,yV​) of a parabola and compute it from the standard form.

  • You can determine whether a parabola opens upwards or downwards from the sign of the leading coefficient aaa.

  • You can compute the zeros of a quadratic function using the pq-formula or the abc-formula.

  • You know the discriminant and can use it to determine the number of real zeros.

  • You can sketch a parabola from its key features.

A parabolic rail profile¶

Imagine we replace the straight inclined rail of our marble track with a curved one that dips into a valley. The rail descends from the starting block, reaches its lowest point somewhere in the middle, and then rises again toward the finishing block. If we place the coordinate origin at the starting block as before, the height function of such a rail might look like this:

h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6.h(x) = 0.01x^2 - 0.5x + 6.h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6.

Here hhh is measured in centimetres and xxx runs from 0 to 50 cm50~\text{cm}50 cm. At x=0x = 0x=0 we have h(0)=6 cmh(0) = 6~\text{cm}h(0)=6 cm, the starting height. The rail dips down, reaches its lowest point, and then climbs back up. The graph of this function is a parabola opening upward, since the coefficient of x2x^2x2 is positive.

A quadratic function of this form, a sum of a degree-2 power function, a linear term, and a constant, is the general shape we want to study.

What is ... a quadratic function?

A quadratic function is a function of the form

f(x)=ax2+bx+c,f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c,f(x)=ax2+bx+c,

where aaa, bbb, and ccc are real numbers and a≠0a \neq 0a=0. This representation is called the standard form. The coefficient aaa is the leading coefficient.

The leading coefficient aaa determines the opening direction of the parabola: if a>0a > 0a>0 the parabola opens upward and has a lowest point; if a<0a < 0a<0 it opens downward and has a highest point. The larger ∣a∣|a|∣a∣ is, the narrower the parabola.

Standard form and vertex form¶

The standard form f(x)=ax2+bx+cf(x) = ax^2 + bx + cf(x)=ax2+bx+c is convenient for reading off the yyy-intercept directly: setting x=0x = 0x=0 gives f(0)=cf(0) = cf(0)=c. However, it does not immediately reveal the most important geometric feature of the parabola: its vertex.

The vertex V=(xV,yV)V = (x_V, y_V)V=(xV​,yV​) is the lowest point of an upward-opening parabola or the highest point of a downward-opening one. It is the point where the parabola changes from decreasing to increasing (or vice versa). We can compute the xxx-coordinate of the vertex from the standard form by completing the square, which gives:

xV=−b2a.x_V = -\frac{b}{2a}.xV​=−2ab​.

Once we know xVx_VxV​, the yyy-coordinate follows by substitution:

yV=f(xV)=axV2+bxV+c.y_V = f(x_V) = a x_V^2 + b x_V + c.yV​=f(xV​)=axV2​+bxV​+c.

For our parabolic rail h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6h(x) = 0.01x^2 - 0.5x + 6h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6 we obtain:

xV=−−0.52⋅0.01=0.50.02=25 cm,x_V = -\frac{-0.5}{2 \cdot 0.01} = \frac{0.5}{0.02} = 25~\text{cm},xV​=−2⋅0.01−0.5​=0.020.5​=25 cm,
yV=0.01⋅252−0.5⋅25+6=6.25−12.5+6=−0.25 cm.y_V = 0.01 \cdot 25^2 - 0.5 \cdot 25 + 6 = 6.25 - 12.5 + 6 = -0.25~\text{cm}.yV​=0.01⋅252−0.5⋅25+6=6.25−12.5+6=−0.25 cm.

The lowest point of the rail lies at x=25 cmx = 25~\text{cm}x=25 cm, at a height of −0.25 cm-0.25~\text{cm}−0.25 cm, that is, just below the table surface. Knowing the vertex, we can write the height function in the alternative vertex form:

What is ... the vertex form?

The vertex form of a quadratic function is

f(x)=a(x−xV)2+yV,f(x) = a(x - x_V)^2 + y_V,f(x)=a(x−xV​)2+yV​,

where V=(xV,yV)V = (x_V, y_V)V=(xV​,yV​) is the vertex of the parabola and aaa is the leading coefficient. The vertex form makes the position of the vertex immediately visible.

Both forms describe exactly the same function. The standard form is convenient for calculations involving individual coefficients; the vertex form is convenient whenever the vertex itself is the quantity of interest.

Zeros and the discriminant¶

Where does the parabolic rail cross the table surface? This is the question about the zeros of the height function, that is, the values of xxx for which h(x)=0h(x) = 0h(x)=0. For our rail, a zero corresponds to a point where the ball is exactly at table height, which is physically the point where it either leaves or re-enters the table surface.

For a quadratic function in the special form f(x)=x2+px+qf(x) = x^2 + px + qf(x)=x2+px+q (that is, with leading coefficient a=1a = 1a=1), we use the pq-formula:

x1,2=−p2±(p2)2−q.x_{1,2} = -\frac{p}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{p}{2}\right)^2 - q}.x1,2​=−2p​±(2p​)2−q​.

For the general standard form f(x)=ax2+bx+cf(x) = ax^2 + bx + cf(x)=ax2+bx+c with arbitrary a≠0a \neq 0a=0, we use the quadratic formula:

x1,2=−b±b2−4ac2a.x_{1,2} = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}.x1,2​=2a−b±b2−4ac​​.

Both formulas are equivalent: dividing the standard form through by aaa and setting p=b/ap = b/ap=b/a and q=c/aq = c/aq=c/a converts one into the other.

The expression under the square root is called the discriminant. For the quadratic formula it is Δ=b2−4ac\Delta = b^2 - 4acΔ=b2−4ac, and it tells us immediately how many real zeros the quadratic function has:

  • Δ>0\Delta > 0Δ>0: two distinct real zeros (the parabola crosses the xxx-axis twice).

  • Δ=0\Delta = 0Δ=0: exactly one real zero (the parabola touches the xxx-axis at the vertex).

  • Δ<0\Delta < 0Δ<0: no real zeros (the parabola lies entirely above or below the xxx-axis).

The case Δ=0\Delta = 0Δ=0 has a neat geometric interpretation: the vertex lies exactly on the xxx-axis, so yV=0y_V = 0yV​=0.

Let us apply this to our parabolic rail h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6h(x) = 0.01x^2 - 0.5x + 6h(x)=0.01x2−0.5x+6. We compute the discriminant:

Δ=(−0.5)2−4⋅0.01⋅6=0.25−0.24=0.01>0.\Delta = (-0.5)^2 - 4 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 6 = 0.25 - 0.24 = 0.01 > 0.Δ=(−0.5)2−4⋅0.01⋅6=0.25−0.24=0.01>0.

Since Δ>0\Delta > 0Δ>0, there are two zeros. Using the quadratic formula:

x1,2=0.5±0.012⋅0.01=0.5±0.10.02,x_{1,2} = \frac{0.5 \pm \sqrt{0.01}}{2 \cdot 0.01} = \frac{0.5 \pm 0.1}{0.02},x1,2​=2⋅0.010.5±0.01​​=0.020.5±0.1​,

which gives x1=30 cmx_1 = 30~\text{cm}x1​=30 cm and x2=20 cmx_2 = 20~\text{cm}x2​=20 cm. The rail crosses the table surface at two positions, consistent with the vertex being just below the surface at yS=−0.25 cmy_S = -0.25~\text{cm}yS​=−0.25 cm.

Sketching a parabola¶

With the tools developed above we can sketch any parabola quickly and accurately. We proceed in four steps:

  1. Opening direction: check the sign of aaa. If a>0a > 0a>0, the parabola opens upward; if a<0a < 0a<0, it opens downward.

  2. Vertex: compute xV=−b/(2a)x_V = -b/(2a)xV​=−b/(2a) and yV=f(xV)y_V = f(x_V)yV​=f(xV​). Mark the vertex V=(xV,yV)V = (x_V, y_V)V=(xV​,yV​) in the coordinate system.

  3. Zeros: compute the discriminant Δ=b2−4ac\Delta = b^2 - 4acΔ=b2−4ac. If Δ>0\Delta > 0Δ>0, compute and mark both zeros using the quadratic formula. If Δ=0\Delta = 0Δ=0, the single zero is the xxx-coordinate of the vertex. If Δ<0\Delta < 0Δ<0, there are no zeros to mark.

  4. yyy-intercept: compute f(0)=cf(0) = cf(0)=c and mark the point (0,c)(0, c)(0,c).

These four pieces of information are sufficient for a qualitatively correct sketch. A smooth curve through the vertex, symmetric about the vertical line x=xVx = x_Vx=xV​, connecting the zeros and the yyy-intercept, completes the drawing.

The vertex and differential calculus¶

We have seen that the vertex is the point where the parabola transitions from decreasing to increasing or vice versa. At that point the rail is neither rising nor falling: its steepness is momentarily zero. In the language of differential calculus, the derivative of the function is zero at the vertex.

This observation is not specific to parabolas. For any differentiable function, the points where the derivative is zero are candidates for local minima and maxima. Finding these points is one of the central tasks of differential calculus, and the vertex of a parabola is the simplest possible example. We will return to this idea in detail in a later chapter.

Summary and outlook¶

The quadratic function has two equivalent representations: the standard form, which shows the yyy-intercept directly, and the vertex form, which shows the vertex directly. The discriminant determines the number of real zeros. Taken together, the opening direction, vertex, zeros, and yyy-intercept are sufficient to sketch any parabola.
