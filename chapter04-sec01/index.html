Exponential Functions

In chapter 3 we built up a toolkit of polynomial functions by combining power functions. Polynomials are very flexible, but they cannot describe one of the most common behaviors in engineering: processes that grow or decay at a rate proportional to their current value. A capacitor charging through a resistor, a hot component cooling down, a vibration losing energy to friction: all of these follow the same mathematical pattern, and none of them can be captured by a polynomial. To describe them we need the exponential function.

The capacitor charging curve¶

Imagine a simple electrical circuit consisting of a resistor RRR and a capacitor CCC connected in series to a supply voltage U0U_0U0​. At time t=0t = 0t=0 the circuit is switched on. The voltage across the capacitor is initially zero and rises as charge accumulates on the capacitor plates. Our question is: How does the voltage across the capacitor change over time?

UC(t)=U0(1−e−t/τ),U_C(t) = U_0 \left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right),UC​(t)=U0​(1−e−t/τ),

where τ=RC\tau = RCτ=RC is the time constant of the circuit, measured in seconds. At t=0t = 0t=0 we have UC(0)=0U_C(0) = 0UC​(0)=0, as expected. As ttt increases, the voltage rises and approaches U0U_0U0​ asymptotically: the capacitor never quite reaches full charge, but gets arbitrarily close.

The time constant τ\tauτ controls how fast this happens. After one time constant (t=τt = \taut=τ), the voltage has reached approximately 63%63\%63% of the supply voltage. After five time constants (t=5τt = 5\taut=5τ), it is within 1%1\%1% of U0U_0U0​, which is close enough to consider the capacitor fully charged in practice.

The reverse process, discharging a fully charged capacitor through a resistor, gives the curve:

UC(t)=U0⋅e−t/τ.U_C(t) = U_0 \cdot e^{-t/\tau}.UC​(t)=U0​⋅e−t/τ.

Here the voltage starts at U0U_0U0​ and decays toward zero. Both curves are built around the same core: the expression e−t/τe^{-t/\tau}e−t/τ, an exponential function of time.

These curves appear throughout engineering: the temperature of a cooling component, the amplitude of a damped oscillation, the concentration of a substance undergoing radioactive decay, and the speed of a vehicle braking against air resistance all follow the same exponential pattern. Understanding the exponential function is therefore not optional for an engineer.

Definition and properties¶

The general form of the exponential function is f(x)=axf(x) = a^xf(x)=ax, where the base aaa is a positive real number different from 1. We require a>0a > 0a>0 because raising a negative number to an arbitrary real exponent is not defined in general. We require a≠1a \neq 1a=1 because 1x=11^x = 11x=1 for all xxx, which gives only the constant function.

What is ... an exponential function?

An exponential function is a function of the form

f(x)=ax,f(x) = a^x,f(x)=ax,

where the base aaa satisfies a>0a > 0a>0 and a≠1a \neq 1a=1. The variable xxx is the exponent.

The key properties of the exponential function follow directly from its definition. They are worth comparing with the polynomial properties we studied in chapter 3.

Domain and range. The domain is all of R\mathbb{R}R: we can raise a positive number to any real power. The range is (0,∞)(0, \infty)(0,∞): the exponential function is always strictly positive. In particular, ax=0a^x = 0ax=0 has no solution, so the exponential function has no zeros.

The point (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1). Since a0=1a^0 = 1a0=1 for any base aaa, every exponential function passes through the point (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1).

Monotonicity. If a>1a > 1a>1, the function is strictly monotonically increasing: larger exponents give larger values. If 0<a<10 < a < 10<a<1, the function is strictly monotonically decreasing: larger exponents give smaller values. The charging curve uses a=e>1a = e > 1a=e>1 (increasing), while the discharging curve uses the same base but with a negative exponent, which is equivalent to using a base less than 1 (decreasing).

Boundedness. For a>1a > 1a>1 the function is bounded below by 0 but unbounded above: as x→+∞x \to +\inftyx→+∞ the function grows without bound. For 0<a<10 < a < 10<a<1 the function is also bounded below by 0 but unbounded above: as x→−∞x \to -\inftyx→−∞ the function grows without bound. In both cases the value 0 is never reached, since ax>0a^x > 0ax>0 for all xxx.

Symmetry. The exponential function is neither even nor odd. It has no symmetry about the yyy-axis or the origin.

Euler’s number and the natural exponential function¶

Among all possible bases, one stands out as the natural choice for science and engineering: Euler’s number,

e=2.71828 18284 59045 …e = 2.71828\,18284\,59045\,\ldotse=2.718281828459045…

This number is irrational, like π\piπ, and it appears in an enormous range of mathematical and physical contexts. Its defining property, which we will appreciate fully when we study differentiation, is the following: the function f(x)=exf(x) = e^xf(x)=ex is the only exponential function whose rate of change at every point equals its own value at that point. In other words, the slope of the graph of exe^xex at any point xxx is exactly exe^xex. No other base has this property.

This self-referential property makes exe^xex the natural building block for describing processes where the rate of change is proportional to the current value, which is precisely the situation in our RC circuit: the charging current is proportional to the remaining voltage difference, and the result is the exponential charging curve.

What is ... the natural exponential function?

The natural exponential function is f(x)=exf(x) = e^xf(x)=ex, where e≈2.71828e \approx 2.71828e≈2.71828 is Euler’s number. It is the standard exponential function used in science and engineering.

In the RC circuit, the time constant τ\tauτ appears in the exponent as e−t/τe^{-t/\tau}e−t/τ. This is the natural exponential function evaluated at −t/τ-t/\tau−t/τ, that is, a composition of the form f(g(t))f(g(t))f(g(t)) with inner function g(t)=−t/τg(t) = -t/\taug(t)=−t/τ and outer function f(u)=euf(u) = e^uf(u)=eu. Calculation rules¶

Working with exponential expressions requires a set of algebraic rules. These rules follow directly from the definition of exponentiation and hold for any base a>0a > 0a>0, a≠1a \neq 1a=1.

Product rule. When multiplying two exponentials with the same base, we add the exponents:

am⋅an=am+n.a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}.am⋅an=am+n.

For the RC circuit: e−t/τ⋅e−s/τ=e−(t+s)/τe^{-t/\tau} \cdot e^{-s/\tau} = e^{-(t+s)/\tau}e−t/τ⋅e−s/τ=e−(t+s)/τ.

Quotient rule. When dividing two exponentials with the same base, we subtract the exponents:

aman=am−n.\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}.anam​=am−n.

Power rule. When raising an exponential to a further power, we multiply the exponents:

(am)n=am⋅n.(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}.(am)n=am⋅n.

Reciprocal. A negative exponent gives the reciprocal:

a−n=1an.a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}.a−n=an1​.

This is why the discharging curve e−t/τe^{-t/\tau}e−t/τ decreases toward zero: as ttt grows, −t/τ-t/\tau−t/τ becomes more and more negative, and e−t/τ=1/et/τe^{-t/\tau} = 1/e^{t/\tau}e−t/τ=1/et/τ approaches zero from above.

Different bases. The rules above apply when the bases are the same. To convert between different bases, we use the identity:

ax=exln⁡a,a^x = e^{x \ln a},ax=exlna,

where ln⁡\lnln is the natural logarithm, which we will introduce in section 4.3.

Summary and outlook¶

The exponential function describes processes where a quantity grows or decays at a rate proportional to its current value. Its key features are a domain of all real numbers, a strictly positive range, no zeros, and monotonic behavior controlled by the base. Among all exponential functions, exe^xex is the natural choice for science and engineering because of its unique property in differentiation. The calculation rules allow us to simplify and manipulate exponential expressions algebraically. The capacitor charging curve raises a natural question: if we know the voltage and want to find the time at which it was reached, we need to solve an equation of the form e−t/τ=ce^{-t/\tau} = ce−t/τ=c for ttt. This requires a function that undoes the exponential, and building that function is the subject of the next section.
