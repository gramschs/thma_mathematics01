4.3 Logarithmic Functions

At the end of the previous section we were left with an equation we could not yet solve: e−t/τ=0.5e^{-t/\tau} = 0.5e−t/τ=0.5. We know that the exponential function is invertible, and we know how to find inverse functions in principle. Now we carry out that process for the exponential function explicitly. The result is the logarithm, one of the most frequently used functions in science and engineering.

Learning goals¶

Learning goals

  • You know the definition of the logarithm log⁡a(x)\log_a(x)loga​(x) as the inverse of the exponential function with base aaa.

  • You are familiar with the natural logarithm ln⁡(x)=log⁡e(x)\ln(x) = \log_e(x)ln(x)=loge​(x) and know its key properties: domain, range, zeros, monotonicity, and symmetry.

  • You can sketch the graph of the natural logarithm.

  • You know the calculation rules for logarithms and can apply them.

  • You can solve equations of the form ax=ba^x = bax=b using the logarithm.

The logarithm as the inverse of the exponential function¶

We return to the capacitor discharge equation and the question from the previous section: for which time ttt does the voltage drop to half its initial value? We had reduced this to:

e−t/τ=0.5.e^{-t/\tau} = 0.5.e−t/τ=0.5.

Following the four-step procedure from the previous section, we write y=exy = e^xy=ex and solve for xxx. The result is a new function: the value of xxx for which exe^xex equals a given positive number yyy. We call this the natural logarithm of yyy and write it as x=ln⁡(y)x = \ln(y)x=ln(y).

With this notation, we can immediately answer the capacitor question. From e−t/τ=0.5e^{-t/\tau} = 0.5e−t/τ=0.5 we take the natural logarithm of both sides:

−tτ=ln⁡(0.5)≈−0.693.-\frac{t}{\tau} = \ln(0.5) \approx -0.693.−τt​=ln(0.5)≈−0.693.

Multiplying both sides by −τ-\tau−τ:

t=τ⋅ln⁡(2)≈0.693⋅τ.t = \tau \cdot \ln(2) \approx 0.693 \cdot \tau.t=τ⋅ln(2)≈0.693⋅τ.

The voltage drops to half its initial value after approximately 0.693 time constants. This quantity appears so often in physics and engineering that it has its own name: the half-life of the decay process.

The natural logarithm is the inverse function of exe^xex, which means the two functions undo each other completely:

ln⁡(ex)=xfor all x∈R,\ln(e^x) = x \quad \text{for all } x \in \mathbb{R},ln(ex)=xfor all x∈R,
eln⁡(x)=xfor all x>0.e^{\ln(x)} = x \quad \text{for all } x > 0.eln(x)=xfor all x>0.

More generally, the logarithm to base aaa is defined as the inverse of the exponential function with base aaa.

What is ... the logarithm?

Let a>0a > 0a>0, a≠1a \neq 1a=1. The logarithm to base aaa of a positive number xxx is the exponent to which aaa must be raised to obtain xxx:

log⁡a(x)=yif and only ifay=x.\log_a(x) = y \quad \text{if and only if} \quad a^y = x.loga​(x)=yif and only ifay=x.

The natural logarithm is the logarithm to base eee:

ln⁡(x)=log⁡e(x).\ln(x) = \log_e(x).ln(x)=loge​(x).

Properties of the natural logarithm¶

Since ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x) is the inverse of exe^xex, its graph is the reflection of the graph of exe^xex about the line y=xy = xy=x. From this reflection we can read off all the key properties directly, using the fact that domain and range swap when a function is inverted.

Domain. The exponential function exe^xex has range (0,∞)(0, \infty)(0,∞), so the natural logarithm has domain (0,∞)(0, \infty)(0,∞). We cannot take the logarithm of zero or of a negative number. This is not a formal restriction imposed from outside: it reflects a genuine absence of a real solution to ey=xe^y = xey=x when x≤0x \leq 0x≤0, since eye^yey is always strictly positive.

Range. The exponential function has domain R\mathbb{R}R, so the natural logarithm has range R\mathbb{R}R. For every real number yyy, no matter how large or how negative, there exists a positive xxx with ln⁡(x)=y\ln(x) = yln(x)=y.

Zero. Since e0=1e^0 = 1e0=1, we have ln⁡(1)=0\ln(1) = 0ln(1)=0. The graph of the natural logarithm crosses the xxx-axis at exactly one point: x=1x = 1x=1.

Monotonicity. Since exe^xex is strictly monotonically increasing, so is its inverse ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x): larger inputs give larger outputs.

Boundedness. As x→∞x \to \inftyx→∞, ln⁡(x)→∞\ln(x) \to \inftyln(x)→∞: the natural logarithm is unbounded above. As x→0+x \to 0^+x→0+, ln⁡(x)→−∞\ln(x) \to -\inftyln(x)→−∞: the natural logarithm is unbounded below. The yyy-axis is a vertical asymptote. The function is therefore neither bounded above nor below.

Symmetry. The natural logarithm is neither even nor odd.

Calculation rules for logarithms¶

The calculation rules for logarithms correspond directly to the calculation rules for exponentials from the previous section. Each logarithm rule can be derived from the corresponding exponential rule by applying ln⁡\lnln to both sides and using the identity ln⁡(ex)=x\ln(e^x) = xln(ex)=x.

Product rule. The logarithm of a product equals the sum of the logarithms:

ln⁡(a⋅b)=ln⁡(a)+ln⁡(b).\ln(a \cdot b) = \ln(a) + \ln(b).ln(a⋅b)=ln(a)+ln(b).

This follows from the product rule for exponentials: if a=ema = e^ma=em and b=enb = e^nb=en, then a⋅b=em+na \cdot b = e^{m+n}a⋅b=em+n, so ln⁡(a⋅b)=m+n=ln⁡(a)+ln⁡(b)\ln(a \cdot b) = m + n = \ln(a) + \ln(b)ln(a⋅b)=m+n=ln(a)+ln(b).

Quotient rule. The logarithm of a quotient equals the difference of the logarithms:

ln⁡ ⁣(ab)=ln⁡(a)−ln⁡(b).\ln\!\left(\frac{a}{b}\right) = \ln(a) - \ln(b).ln(ba​)=ln(a)−ln(b).

Power rule. The logarithm of a power equals the exponent times the logarithm:

ln⁡(an)=n⋅ln⁡(a).\ln(a^n) = n \cdot \ln(a).ln(an)=n⋅ln(a).

This rule is particularly useful for solving exponential equations: it brings the unknown exponent down from the power to a factor, where it can be isolated by ordinary algebra.

Let us apply these rules to solve a general exponential equation. Suppose we want to find xxx such that 2x=102^x = 102x=10. Taking the natural logarithm of both sides and applying the power rule:

x⋅ln⁡(2)=ln⁡(10),x \cdot \ln(2) = \ln(10),x⋅ln(2)=ln(10),
x=ln⁡(10)ln⁡(2)≈2.3030.693≈3.32.x = \frac{\ln(10)}{\ln(2)} \approx \frac{2.303}{0.693} \approx 3.32.x=ln(2)ln(10)​≈0.6932.303​≈3.32.

We can verify: 23.32≈102^{3.32} \approx 1023.32≈10. The same technique applies to any equation of the form ax=ba^x = bax=b with a,b>0a, b > 0a,b>0 and a≠1a \neq 1a=1.

Other logarithms¶

The natural logarithm is the standard choice in mathematics and engineering, but two other logarithms appear often enough to deserve mention.

The common logarithm log⁡10(x)\log_{10}(x)log10​(x) uses base 10. It appears in the definition of the decibel scale for sound and signal levels, in the pH scale for acidity, and in the Richter scale for earthquake magnitudes. The common logarithm is sometimes written simply as log⁡(x)\log(x)log(x) without a base, especially in engineering contexts.

The binary logarithm log⁡2(x)\log_2(x)log2​(x) uses base 2. It appears in information theory, where the amount of information in a message is measured in bits, and in computer science, where algorithms are analyzed by how many times a problem can be halved before it is solved.

Any logarithm can be converted to the natural logarithm using the change of base formula. For any base a>0a > 0a>0, a≠1a \neq 1a=1:

log⁡a(x)=ln⁡(x)ln⁡(a).\log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)}.loga​(x)=ln(a)ln(x)​.

This formula follows directly from the definition: if log⁡a(x)=y\log_a(x) = yloga​(x)=y, then ay=xa^y = xay=x, so yln⁡(a)=ln⁡(x)y \ln(a) = \ln(x)yln(a)=ln(x), giving y=ln⁡(x)/ln⁡(a)y = \ln(x)/\ln(a)y=ln(x)/ln(a). In practice this means we only need one logarithm on a calculator: the natural logarithm is sufficient to evaluate any other logarithm.

Summary and outlook¶

The natural logarithm is the inverse of the natural exponential function. Its domain is (0,∞)(0, \infty)(0,∞), it has a single zero at x=1x = 1x=1, and it grows without bound but increasingly slowly. The calculation rules for logarithms mirror those for exponentials and are the main tool for solving equations where the unknown appears in an exponent. Together, exe^xex and ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x) form a pair of inverse functions that underpin an enormous range of engineering calculations. We now turn to a completely different family of functions: the trigonometric functions, which describe the oscillations and periodic phenomena that are equally fundamental in mechanical engineering.
