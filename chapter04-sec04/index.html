Trigonometric Functions

A crankshaft rotates, a piston oscillates, a bridge vibrates under traffic load, an alternating voltage drives a motor. Periodic phenomena are everywhere in mechanical engineering, and the mathematical tool for describing all of them is the same: the trigonometric functions. In this section we build these functions from the ground up, starting from the right-angled triangle and extending to the unit circle so that every angle, positive, negative, or larger than a full turn, is covered.

Learning goals¶

Learning goals

  • You know how sine, cosine, and tangent are defined in the right-angled triangle using opposite side, adjacent side, and hypotenuse.

  • You can read off the values of sine and cosine on the unit circle for standard angles and know how to convert between degrees and radians.

  • You know the key properties of sine and cosine: domain, range, period 2π2\pi2π, symmetry, zeros, maxima, and minima.

  • You know the properties of the tangent: domain, range, period π\piπ, zeros, and vertical asymptotes.

  • You can interpret the parameters of the general sinusoidal form f(t)=Asin⁡(ωt+φ)+df(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \varphi) + df(t)=Asin(ωt+φ)+d in an engineering context.

Trigonometric functions in the right-angled triangle¶

Imagine a connecting rod in an engine. One end is fixed at the crankshaft center and the other traces a circular path. To find the horizontal and vertical components of the rod’s position at a given angle, we decompose its length into two perpendicular directions. This decomposition is exactly what sine and cosine do.

Consider a right-angled triangle with an acute angle α\alphaα at one corner. The three sides of the triangle are named relative to the angle α\alphaα:

  • the opposite side is the side across from α\alphaα,

  • the adjacent side is the side that forms one of the two legs of α\alphaα,

  • the hypotenuse is the longest side, opposite the right angle.

We define the three basic trigonometric functions by the ratios of these sides:

sin⁡(α)=oppositehypotenuse,cos⁡(α)=adjacenthypotenuse,tan⁡(α)=oppositeadjacent.\sin(\alpha) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \cos(\alpha) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}, \quad \tan(\alpha) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}.sin(α)=hypotenuseopposite​,cos(α)=hypotenuseadjacent​,tan(α)=adjacentopposite​.

For the connecting rod of length rrr at angle α\alphaα, the horizontal component of its tip is rcos⁡(α)r \cos(\alpha)rcos(α) and the vertical component is rsin⁡(α)r \sin(\alpha)rsin(α). Sine and cosine decompose a length into its components along two perpendicular directions.

This triangle-based definition has one limitation: it only works for angles between 0°0°0° and 90°90°90°, since a right-angled triangle cannot contain an angle greater than 90°90°90°. To describe a full rotation of the crankshaft, including angles beyond 90°90°90° and even multiple complete turns, we need a broader definition.

The unit circle¶

We extend the definition of sine and cosine to all angles using the unit circle, a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin. For any angle α\alphaα, we place the angle at the origin with one ray along the positive xxx-axis and measure α\alphaα counterclockwise. The point where the other ray meets the unit circle has coordinates (cos⁡(α),sin⁡(α))(\cos(\alpha), \sin(\alpha))(cos(α),sin(α)).

This definition agrees with the triangle definition for 0<α<90°0 < \alpha < 90°0<α<90° and extends it naturally to all other angles. A negative angle is measured clockwise. An angle greater than 360°360°360° simply means more than one full rotation and gives the same point on the circle as the corresponding angle reduced by the appropriate multiple of 360°360°360°.

Degrees and radians. In the triangle definition, angles are naturally measured in degrees. In calculus and most engineering formulas, angles are measured in radians. One full rotation is 2π2\pi2π radians, so 180°=π180° = \pi180°=π radians. The conversion formulas are:

αrad=π180°⋅αdeg,αdeg=180°π⋅αrad.\alpha_{\text{rad}} = \frac{\pi}{180°} \cdot \alpha_{\text{deg}}, \qquad \alpha_{\text{deg}} = \frac{180°}{\pi} \cdot \alpha_{\text{rad}}.αrad​=180°π​⋅αdeg​,αdeg​=π180°​⋅αrad​.

From here on we use radians exclusively.

Properties of sine and cosine¶

With the unit circle definition in hand, we can determine all the key properties of sine and cosine.

Domain and range. Every angle α∈R\alpha \in \mathbb{R}α∈R corresponds to a point on the unit circle, so the domain of both sine and cosine is R\mathbb{R}R. The coordinates of any point on the unit circle lie between -1 and 1, so the range of both functions is [−1,1][-1, 1][−1,1].

Period. Adding 2π2\pi2π to an angle returns to the same point on the unit circle: sin⁡(α+2π)=sin⁡(α)\sin(\alpha + 2\pi) = \sin(\alpha)sin(α+2π)=sin(α) and cos⁡(α+2π)=cos⁡(α)\cos(\alpha + 2\pi) = \cos(\alpha)cos(α+2π)=cos(α). Both functions are periodic with period T=2πT = 2\piT=2π, and 2π2\pi2π is the fundamental period.

Symmetry. Cosine is an even function: cos⁡(−α)=cos⁡(α)\cos(-\alpha) = \cos(\alpha)cos(−α)=cos(α). Geometrically, reflecting an angle about the xxx-axis does not change the xxx-coordinate of the corresponding unit circle point. Sine is an odd function: sin⁡(−α)=−sin⁡(α)\sin(-\alpha) = -\sin(\alpha)sin(−α)=−sin(α). Reflecting about the xxx-axis changes the sign of the yyy-coordinate.

Zeros, maxima, and minima. The zeros of sine are at α=kπ\alpha = k\piα=kπ for any integer kkk. Its maxima of value 1 occur at α=π/2+2kπ\alpha = \pi/2 + 2k\piα=π/2+2kπ and its minima of value -1 at α=−π/2+2kπ\alpha = -\pi/2 + 2k\piα=−π/2+2kπ. The zeros of cosine are at α=π/2+kπ\alpha = \pi/2 + k\piα=π/2+kπ. Its maxima of value 1 occur at α=2kπ\alpha = 2k\piα=2kπ and its minima of value -1 at α=π+2kπ\alpha = \pi + 2k\piα=π+2kπ.

The tangent¶

The tangent is defined as the ratio of sine to cosine:

tan⁡(α)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(α).\tan(\alpha) = \frac{\sin(\alpha)}{\cos(\alpha)}.tan(α)=cos(α)sin(α)​.

This is consistent with the triangle definition:

sin⁡(α)cos⁡(α)=(opposite/hypotenuse)(adjacent/hypotenuse)=oppositeadjacent.\frac{\sin(\alpha)}{\cos(\alpha)} = \frac{(\text{opposite}/\text{hypotenuse})}{(\text{adjacent}/\text{hypotenuse})} = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}.cos(α)sin(α)​=(adjacent/hypotenuse)(opposite/hypotenuse)​=adjacentopposite​.

Domain. Since the denominator cos⁡(α)\cos(\alpha)cos(α) is zero at α=π/2+kπ\alpha = \pi/2 + k\piα=π/2+kπ for integer kkk, the tangent is not defined at these points. The domain is R∖{π/2+kπ∣k∈Z}\mathbb{R} \setminus \{\pi/2 + k\pi \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}\}R∖{π/2+kπ∣k∈Z}.

Range. As α\alphaα approaches π/2\pi/2π/2 from below, cos⁡(α)\cos(\alpha)cos(α) approaches zero from above while sin⁡(α)\sin(\alpha)sin(α) approaches 1, so tan⁡(α)→+∞\tan(\alpha) \to +\inftytan(α)→+∞. Approaching π/2\pi/2π/2 from above gives tan⁡(α)→−∞\tan(\alpha) \to -\inftytan(α)→−∞. The tangent therefore takes all real values: range R\mathbb{R}R.

Period. Since sin⁡(α+π)=−sin⁡(α)\sin(\alpha + \pi) = -\sin(\alpha)sin(α+π)=−sin(α) and cos⁡(α+π)=−cos⁡(α)\cos(\alpha + \pi) = -\cos(\alpha)cos(α+π)=−cos(α), their ratio satisfies tan⁡(α+π)=tan⁡(α)\tan(\alpha + \pi) = \tan(\alpha)tan(α+π)=tan(α). The tangent has period π\piπ, half the period of sine and cosine.

Zeros and asymptotes. The tangent is zero wherever sine is zero: at α=kπ\alpha = k\piα=kπ. The vertical asymptotes occur at α=π/2+kπ\alpha = \pi/2 + k\piα=π/2+kπ, the points excluded from the domain.

The general sinusoidal form¶

In engineering applications, the oscillation we want to describe is rarely a pure sine with amplitude 1 and no phase offset. An alternating voltage, for instance, has a specific amplitude, a specific frequency, and a specific phase relative to some reference signal. The general sinusoidal form

f(t)=Asin⁡(ωt+φ)+df(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \varphi) + df(t)=Asin(ωt+φ)+d

captures all of these features through four parameters.

Amplitude AAA: the maximum deviation from the center value. The function oscillates between d−∣A∣d - |A|d−∣A∣ and d+∣A∣d + |A|d+∣A∣. For a household alternating voltage in Europe, A≈325 VA \approx 325~\text{V}A≈325 V.

Angular frequency ω\omegaω: controls how fast the oscillation repeats. The period is T=2π/ωT = 2\pi/\omegaT=2π/ω. The ordinary frequency in Hz is f=1/T=ω/(2π)f = 1/T = \omega/(2\pi)f=1/T=ω/(2π). For European mains voltage, f=50 Hzf = 50~\text{Hz}f=50 Hz, so ω=100π rad/s\omega = 100\pi~\text{rad/s}ω=100π rad/s.

Phase shift φ\varphiφ: shifts the entire curve horizontally. A positive φ\varphiφ shifts the curve to the left by φ/ω\varphi/\omegaφ/ω. When two signals have the same frequency but different phase shifts, we say they are out of phase. Phase differences are critical in the analysis of electrical circuits and mechanical vibrations.

Vertical offset ddd: shifts the entire curve up or down. For a pure AC signal, d=0d = 0d=0. For a signal with a DC component superimposed on an oscillation, d≠0d \neq 0d=0.

Summary and outlook¶

The trigonometric functions extend the triangle definitions of sine, cosine, and tangent to all real angles via the unit circle. Sine and cosine share the domain R\mathbb{R}R, range [−1,1][-1, 1][−1,1], and period 2π2\pi2π, but differ in symmetry: cosine is even, sine is odd. The tangent has a smaller domain due to its vertical asymptotes and period π\piπ. The general sinusoidal form with its four parameters is the standard model for periodic signals in engineering. In the next section we study the relationships between the trigonometric functions, including the Pythagorean identity and the addition theorems, which are the main algebraic tools for working with these functions.
