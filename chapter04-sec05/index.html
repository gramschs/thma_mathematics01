Trigonometric Identities

We now have the graphs and properties of sine, cosine, and tangent firmly in hand. The next step is to understand the algebraic relationships between them. These relationships, known as trigonometric identities, are the tools we will reach for whenever we need to simplify a trigonometric expression, rewrite an integral into a manageable form, or combine two sinusoidal signals into one.

Learning goals¶

Learning goals

  • You know the Pythagorean identity sin⁡2(α)+cos⁡2(α)=1\sin^2(\alpha) + \cos^2(\alpha) = 1sin2(α)+cos2(α)=1 and can apply it to simplify expressions.

  • You know the addition theorems for sine and cosine and can apply them:

    sin⁡(α±β)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(β)±cos⁡(α)sin⁡(β),\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) \pm \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta),sin(α±β)=sin(α)cos(β)±cos(α)sin(β),
    cos⁡(α±β)=cos⁡(α)cos⁡(β)∓sin⁡(α)sin⁡(β).\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) \mp \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta).cos(α±β)=cos(α)cos(β)∓sin(α)sin(β).

  • You can derive the double angle formulas from the addition theorems.

The relationship between sine and cosine¶

Looking at the graphs of sine and cosine from the previous section, we notice something striking: the two curves have exactly the same shape. The cosine graph looks like the sine graph shifted to the left by π/2\pi/2π/2. We can verify this by reading off the unit circle.

For any angle α\alphaα, the point on the unit circle is (cos⁡(α),sin⁡(α))(\cos(\alpha), \sin(\alpha))(cos(α),sin(α)). Now consider the angle α+π/2\alpha + \pi/2α+π/2: we have rotated a further quarter turn counterclockwise. The xxx-coordinate of the new point is cos⁡(α+π/2)\cos(\alpha + \pi/2)cos(α+π/2). But rotating a point (cos⁡(α),sin⁡(α))(\cos(\alpha), \sin(\alpha))(cos(α),sin(α)) by π/2\pi/2π/2 counterclockwise gives the point (−sin⁡(α),cos⁡(α))(-\sin(\alpha), \cos(\alpha))(−sin(α),cos(α)). Reading off the xxx-coordinate:

cos⁡ ⁣(α+π2)=−sin⁡(α).\cos\!\left(\alpha + \frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -\sin(\alpha).cos(α+2π​)=−sin(α).

Reading off the yyy-coordinate of the same rotated point:

sin⁡ ⁣(α+π2)=cos⁡(α).\sin\!\left(\alpha + \frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \cos(\alpha).sin(α+2π​)=cos(α).

Rewriting the second identity with α\alphaα replaced by α−π/2\alpha - \pi/2α−π/2:

sin⁡(α)=cos⁡ ⁣(α−π2).\sin(\alpha) = \cos\!\left(\alpha - \frac{\pi}{2}\right).sin(α)=cos(α−2π​).

These two relationships,

cos⁡(α)=sin⁡ ⁣(α+π2),sin⁡(α)=cos⁡ ⁣(α−π2),\cos(\alpha) = \sin\!\left(\alpha + \frac{\pi}{2}\right), \qquad \sin(\alpha) = \cos\!\left(\alpha - \frac{\pi}{2}\right),cos(α)=sin(α+2π​),sin(α)=cos(α−2π​),

tell us that sine and cosine are the same function, just shifted by a quarter period relative to each other. In signal analysis this shift is called a phase difference of π/2\pi/2π/2, or equivalently 90°90°90°. Two sinusoidal signals that differ only by this shift are said to be in quadrature.

The Pythagorean identity¶

Every point on the unit circle lies at distance 1 from the origin. A point on the unit circle has coordinates (cos⁡(α),sin⁡(α))(\cos(\alpha), \sin(\alpha))(cos(α),sin(α)). Applying the distance formula gives:

cos⁡2(α)+sin⁡2(α)=1.\cos^2(\alpha) + \sin^2(\alpha) = 1.cos2(α)+sin2(α)=1.

This is the Pythagorean identity. It holds for every angle α\alphaα without exception and is probably the most frequently used trigonometric identity in practice.

What is ... the Pythagorean identity?

For every angle α∈R\alpha \in \mathbb{R}α∈R:

sin⁡2(α)+cos⁡2(α)=1.\sin^2(\alpha) + \cos^2(\alpha) = 1.sin2(α)+cos2(α)=1.

Two immediate consequences are worth noting. First, we can express sine in terms of cosine and vice versa:

sin⁡(α)=±1−cos⁡2(α),cos⁡(α)=±1−sin⁡2(α),\sin(\alpha) = \pm\sqrt{1 - \cos^2(\alpha)}, \qquad \cos(\alpha) = \pm\sqrt{1 - \sin^2(\alpha)},sin(α)=±1−cos2(α)​,cos(α)=±1−sin2(α)​,

where the sign depends on the quadrant of α\alphaα. Second, dividing the Pythagorean identity through by cos⁡2(α)\cos^2(\alpha)cos2(α) (for cos⁡(α)≠0\cos(\alpha) \neq 0cos(α)=0) gives an identity for the tangent:

sin⁡2(α)cos⁡2(α)+1=1cos⁡2(α),\frac{\sin^2(\alpha)}{\cos^2(\alpha)} + 1 = \frac{1}{\cos^2(\alpha)},cos2(α)sin2(α)​+1=cos2(α)1​,
tan⁡2(α)+1=1cos⁡2(α).\tan^2(\alpha) + 1 = \frac{1}{\cos^2(\alpha)}.tan2(α)+1=cos2(α)1​.

This identity appears in the computation of certain integrals and in the analysis of vibrating systems.

Addition theorems¶

The addition theorems give the sine and cosine of a sum or difference of two angles in terms of the sines and cosines of the individual angles:

sin⁡(α+β)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(β)+cos⁡(α)sin⁡(β),\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta),sin(α+β)=sin(α)cos(β)+cos(α)sin(β),
sin⁡(α−β)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(β)−cos⁡(α)sin⁡(β),\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta),sin(α−β)=sin(α)cos(β)−cos(α)sin(β),
cos⁡(α+β)=cos⁡(α)cos⁡(β)−sin⁡(α)sin⁡(β),\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta),cos(α+β)=cos(α)cos(β)−sin(α)sin(β),
cos⁡(α−β)=cos⁡(α)cos⁡(β)+sin⁡(α)sin⁡(β).\cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta).cos(α−β)=cos(α)cos(β)+sin(α)sin(β).

These four formulas can be written more compactly using the ± and ∓\mp∓ notation, as in the learning goals, but we write them out in full here for clarity.

A worked example. We use the addition theorem to compute an exact value of sin⁡(75°)=sin⁡(5π/12)\sin(75°) = \sin(5\pi/12)sin(75°)=sin(5π/12). Writing 75°=45°+30°75° = 45° + 30°75°=45°+30°:

sin⁡(75°)=sin⁡(45°+30°)=sin⁡(45°)cos⁡(30°)+cos⁡(45°)sin⁡(30°).\sin(75°) = \sin(45° + 30°) = \sin(45°)\cos(30°) + \cos(45°)\sin(30°).sin(75°)=sin(45°+30°)=sin(45°)cos(30°)+cos(45°)sin(30°).

Substituting the standard values from the previous section:

=22⋅32+22⋅12=64+24=6+24.= \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{4} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{4}.=22​​⋅23​​+22​​⋅21​=46​​+42​​=46​+2​​.

Engineering application. Suppose two sinusoidal signals of the same frequency but different phase shifts are added together:

f(t)=A1sin⁡(ωt+φ1)+A2sin⁡(ωt+φ2).f(t) = A_1 \sin(\omega t + \varphi_1) + A_2 \sin(\omega t + \varphi_2).f(t)=A1​sin(ωt+φ1​)+A2​sin(ωt+φ2​).

Expanding each term using the addition theorem for sine turns this into a sum of terms involving sin⁡(ωt)\sin(\omega t)sin(ωt) and cos⁡(ωt)\cos(\omega t)cos(ωt), which can then be combined into a single sinusoid. This technique, expanding and recombining trigonometric expressions, is fundamental in the analysis of vibrations and electrical circuits.

Double angle formulas¶

Setting β=α\beta = \alphaβ=α in the addition theorems gives the double angle formulas. For sine:

sin⁡(2α)=sin⁡(α+α)=sin⁡(α)cos⁡(α)+cos⁡(α)sin⁡(α)=2sin⁡(α)cos⁡(α).\sin(2\alpha) = \sin(\alpha + \alpha) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha) + \cos(\alpha)\sin(\alpha) = 2\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha).sin(2α)=sin(α+α)=sin(α)cos(α)+cos(α)sin(α)=2sin(α)cos(α).

For cosine there are three equivalent forms, all obtained from cos⁡(2α)=cos⁡2(α)−sin⁡2(α)\cos(2\alpha) = \cos^2(\alpha) - \sin^2(\alpha)cos(2α)=cos2(α)−sin2(α) by applying the Pythagorean identity:

cos⁡(2α)=cos⁡2(α)−sin⁡2(α)=2cos⁡2(α)−1=1−2sin⁡2(α).\cos(2\alpha) = \cos^2(\alpha) - \sin^2(\alpha) = 2\cos^2(\alpha) - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2(\alpha).cos(2α)=cos2(α)−sin2(α)=2cos2(α)−1=1−2sin2(α).

The three forms of the cosine double angle formula are useful in different contexts. Rearranging the second and third forms gives the power reduction formulas:

cos⁡2(α)=1+cos⁡(2α)2,sin⁡2(α)=1−cos⁡(2α)2.\cos^2(\alpha) = \frac{1 + \cos(2\alpha)}{2}, \qquad \sin^2(\alpha) = \frac{1 - \cos(2\alpha)}{2}.cos2(α)=21+cos(2α)​,sin2(α)=21−cos(2α)​.

These formulas replace a squared trigonometric function with an unsquared one at double the angle. They appear constantly in integral calculus: computing the integral of sin⁡2(α)\sin^2(\alpha)sin2(α) or cos⁡2(α)\cos^2(\alpha)cos2(α) directly is awkward, but after applying the power reduction formula the integral becomes straightforward. We will use them in exactly this way when we reach integration.

Summary and outlook¶

The three groups of identities in this section form the core algebraic toolkit for trigonometric calculations. The sine-cosine phase relationship shows that the two functions are the same oscillation viewed from a different starting point. The Pythagorean identity connects the two functions through the geometry of the unit circle and generates further useful identities by simple algebra. The addition theorems allow any sum or difference of angles to be expanded, and the double angle formulas follow as special cases. With these identities, and the power reduction formulas in particular, we are fully prepared for the integration techniques we will encounter in a later chapter. In the final section of this chapter we introduce the hyperbolic functions, which share a striking structural similarity with the trigonometric functions but are built from the exponential function.
