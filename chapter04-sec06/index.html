Limits and Continuity

Throughout this chapter we have used the word “approaches” freely and informally. The discharging capacitor voltage approaches zero without ever reaching it. The natural logarithm approaches negative infinity as its argument approaches zero from the right. In section 4.1 we described exe^xex as the unique exponential function whose rate of change at every point equals its own value, a claim that involves asking what happens to a certain ratio as a time interval shrinks toward zero. Each of these statements rests on the same underlying idea. In this section we make that idea precise through the concept of the limit of a function, and we use it to define the property of continuity, which turns out to be the minimum requirement for the derivative to exist.

What are ... one-sided limits?

The left-hand limit lim⁡x→a−f(x)=L\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = Llimx→a−​f(x)=L means that f(x)f(x)f(x) approaches LLL as xxx approaches aaa from below.

When is a function continuous?¶

The marble track from chapter 3 was physically continuous: the ball rolled along it without ever jumping from one position to another. The mathematical counterpart of this physical intuition is continuity.

The piecewise height function from section 3.3 has no jumps. We verified at both boundary points that the segments connect: h1(8)=h2(8)=4 cmh_1(8) = h_2(8) = 4~\text{cm}h1​(8)=h2​(8)=4 cm and h2(12)=h3(12)=4 cmh_2(12) = h_3(12) = 4~\text{cm}h2​(12)=h3​(12)=4 cm. At x=8 cmx = 8~\text{cm}x=8 cm, the function value h(8)=4 cmh(8) = 4~\text{cm}h(8)=4 cm and the limit lim⁡x→8h(x)=4 cm\lim_{x \to 8} h(x) = 4~\text{cm}limx→8​h(x)=4 cm agree. This is precisely what continuity requires.

What is ... continuity?

A function fff is continuous at a point x=ax = ax=a if three conditions hold:

  • f(a)f(a)f(a) is defined,

  • lim⁡x→af(x)\lim_{x \to a} f(x)limx→a​f(x) exists,

  • lim⁡x→af(x)=f(a)\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)limx→a​f(x)=f(a).

A function is continuous on an interval if it is continuous at every point of that interval.

All the elementary functions we have studied in chapters 3 and 4 are continuous on their respective domains: polynomials, exponential and logarithmic functions, and the trigonometric functions are all continuous wherever they are defined. Discontinuities arise precisely at the points excluded from the domain, such as x=0x = 0x=0 for 1/x1/x1/x, or at the boundaries of piecewise functions where the pieces fail to connect.

There are three common types of discontinuity, each with a different cause.

A removable discontinuity occurs when the limit exists at x=ax = ax=a but either f(a)f(a)f(a) is not defined or f(a)f(a)f(a) differs from the limit. The function sin⁡(x)/x\sin(x)/xsin(x)/x has a removable discontinuity at x=0x = 0x=0: the limit equals 1 but the function is undefined there. The discontinuity can be removed by simply defining f(0)=1f(0) = 1f(0)=1.

A jump discontinuity occurs when both one-sided limits exist but are not equal. The function f(x)=∣x∣/xf(x) = |x|/xf(x)=∣x∣/x has a jump discontinuity at x=0x = 0x=0, as we saw above. In engineering, the closing of an electrical switch produces a jump in the current, and the piecewise stress-strain curve of a material at the yield point is another example.

An infinite discontinuity occurs when the function values grow without bound near x=ax = ax=a. The function f(x)=1/xf(x) = 1/xf(x)=1/x has an infinite discontinuity at x=0x = 0x=0: as x→0+x \to 0^+x→0+ the function grows to +∞+\infty+∞, and as x→0−x \to 0^-x→0− it falls to −∞-\infty−∞. The power functions with negative integer exponents from section 3.4 all share this property.

Summary and outlook¶

The limit of a function captures the value that the function approaches near a point, independently of the function value at that point. One-sided limits allow us to analyze functions that behave differently from the left and from the right, as in the piecewise functions of section 3.3. Two special limits stand out for their importance in what follows: lim⁡x→0sin⁡(x)/x=1\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(x)/x = 1limx→0​sin(x)/x=1 will appear when we differentiate the sine function, and the limit definition of eee connects back to the unique differentiation property of the natural exponential function first described in section 4.1. Continuity is the natural companion to the limit concept: a continuous function is one for which the limit always agrees with the function value. In chapter 5 we will see that continuity at a point is a necessary condition for the derivative to exist there, and the limit notation introduced here will reappear immediately, in the very definition of the derivative.
