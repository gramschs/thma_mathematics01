4.7 Optional: Hyperbolic Functions

A power line hanging between two pylons, a chain suspended between two posts, the curve of a concrete arch bridge designed to carry only compressive loads: all of these shapes are described by the same mathematical curve. It is not a parabola, though it looks like one at first glance. The correct description requires a new family of functions that combines the exponential functions from section 1 in a way that mirrors the structure of the trigonometric functions from sections 4 and 5. These are the hyperbolic functions, and they are the final addition to our toolkit of elementary functions.

Learning goals¶

Learning goals

  • You know the definitions of hyperbolic sine sinh⁡(x)\sinh(x)sinh(x), hyperbolic cosine cosh⁡(x)\cosh(x)cosh(x), and hyperbolic tangent tanh⁡(x)\tanh(x)tanh(x) in terms of the exponential function.

  • You know the key properties of sinh⁡\sinhsinh, cosh⁡\coshcosh, and tanh⁡\tanhtanh: domain, range, zeros, monotonicity, and symmetry.

  • You are familiar with the hyperbolic Pythagorean identity cosh⁡2(x)−sinh⁡2(x)=1\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = 1cosh2(x)−sinh2(x)=1.

Definitions¶

In the previous section we saw that every point on the unit circle can be written as (cos⁡(α),sin⁡(α))(\cos(\alpha), \sin(\alpha))(cos(α),sin(α)). A similar parametric description exists for the unit hyperbola x2−y2=1x^2 - y^2 = 1x2−y2=1: every point on its right branch can be written as (cosh⁡(t),sinh⁡(t))(\cosh(t), \sinh(t))(cosh(t),sinh(t)). This geometric analogy is the origin of the name “hyperbolic functions”.

The definitions are built from the natural exponential function by taking symmetric combinations of exe^xex and e−xe^{-x}e−x:

sinh⁡(x)=ex−e−x2,cosh⁡(x)=ex+e−x2.\sinh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}, \qquad \cosh(x) = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}.sinh(x)=2ex−e−x​,cosh(x)=2ex+e−x​.

The hyperbolic tangent is then defined as their ratio, mirroring the definition of the ordinary tangent:

tanh⁡(x)=sinh⁡(x)cosh⁡(x)=ex−e−xex+e−x.\tanh(x) = \frac{\sinh(x)}{\cosh(x)} = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}.tanh(x)=cosh(x)sinh(x)​=ex+e−xex−e−x​.

Notice the structural parallel with the Euler formulas for the trigonometric functions in terms of complex exponentials coming in a future chapter: cos⁡(x)=(eix+e−ix)/2\cos(x) = (e^{ix} + e^{-ix})/2cos(x)=(eix+e−ix)/2 and sin⁡(x)=(eix−e−ix)/(2i)\sin(x) = (e^{ix} - e^{-ix})/(2i)sin(x)=(eix−e−ix)/(2i). The hyperbolic functions are the real-axis counterparts, using exe^xex and e−xe^{-x}e−x instead of eixe^{ix}eix and e−ixe^{-ix}e−ix.

What are ... the hyperbolic functions?

The hyperbolic sine, hyperbolic cosine, and hyperbolic tangent are defined by:

sinh⁡(x)=ex−e−x2,cosh⁡(x)=ex+e−x2,tanh⁡(x)=ex−e−xex+e−x.\sinh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}, \quad \cosh(x) = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}, \quad \tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}.sinh(x)=2ex−e−x​,cosh(x)=2ex+e−x​,tanh(x)=ex+e−xex−e−x​.

Properties¶

The properties of the hyperbolic functions follow directly from their definitions and from the properties of exe^xex that we established in section 1.

Hyperbolic sine sinh⁡(x)\sinh(x)sinh(x). Since ex−e−x=0e^x - e^{-x} = 0ex−e−x=0 only at x=0x = 0x=0, the only zero is at the origin. For large positive xxx, the term exe^xex dominates and sinh⁡(x)→+∞\sinh(x) \to +\inftysinh(x)→+∞; for large negative xxx, the term −e−x-e^{-x}−e−x dominates and sinh⁡(x)→−∞\sinh(x) \to -\inftysinh(x)→−∞. The function is therefore strictly monotonically increasing with domain R\mathbb{R}R and range R\mathbb{R}R. Checking symmetry: sinh⁡(−x)=(e−x−ex)/2=−sinh⁡(x)\sinh(-x) = (e^{-x} - e^x)/2 = -\sinh(x)sinh(−x)=(e−x−ex)/2=−sinh(x), so sinh⁡\sinhsinh is an odd function, symmetric about the origin.

Hyperbolic cosine cosh⁡(x)\cosh(x)cosh(x). Since ex+e−x≥2e^x + e^{-x} \geq 2ex+e−x≥2 for all xxx (by the AM-GM inequality), we have cosh⁡(x)≥1\cosh(x) \geq 1cosh(x)≥1 for all xxx, with equality at x=0x = 0x=0. The function has no zeros. Its domain is R\mathbb{R}R and its range is [1,∞)[1, \infty)[1,∞). Checking symmetry: cosh⁡(−x)=(e−x+ex)/2=cosh⁡(x)\cosh(-x) = (e^{-x} + e^x)/2 = \cosh(x)cosh(−x)=(e−x+ex)/2=cosh(x), so cosh⁡\coshcosh is an even function, symmetric about the yyy-axis. The minimum at x=0x = 0x=0 has value cosh⁡(0)=1\cosh(0) = 1cosh(0)=1.

Hyperbolic tangent tanh⁡(x)\tanh(x)tanh(x). The zero is at x=0x = 0x=0, where sinh⁡(0)=0\sinh(0) = 0sinh(0)=0. For large positive xxx, both ex−e−xe^x - e^{-x}ex−e−x and ex+e−xe^x + e^{-x}ex+e−x are dominated by exe^xex, so tanh⁡(x)→1\tanh(x) \to 1tanh(x)→1. For large negative xxx, tanh⁡(x)→−1\tanh(x) \to -1tanh(x)→−1. The function is therefore bounded between -1 and 1, with horizontal asymptotes at y=±1y = \pm 1y=±1, domain R\mathbb{R}R, and range (−1,1)(-1, 1)(−1,1). Since tanh⁡(−x)=−tanh⁡(x)\tanh(-x) = -\tanh(x)tanh(−x)=−tanh(x), the function is odd. It is also strictly monotonically increasing.

The tanh⁡\tanhtanh function appears in a surprising range of applications beyond its geometric origin. In mechanics, it describes the velocity of an object falling through a resistive medium: the object accelerates at first and then approaches a terminal velocity asymptotically, exactly the behavior captured by the horizontal asymptotes of tanh⁡\tanhtanh.

Beyond mechanics, tanh⁡\tanhtanh also plays an important role in machine learning, where it is one of the classical activation functions in neural networks. It squashes any real-valued input into the range (−1,1)(-1, 1)(−1,1), which keeps values under control as they pass through layer after layer of a network.

The hyperbolic Pythagorean identity¶

In the previous section, the Pythagorean identity sin⁡2(α)+cos⁡2(α)=1\sin^2(\alpha) + \cos^2(\alpha) = 1sin2(α)+cos2(α)=1 followed from the geometry of the unit circle. The hyperbolic counterpart follows directly from the definitions by straightforward algebra:

cosh⁡2(x)−sinh⁡2(x)=(ex+e−x2)2−(ex−e−x2)2.\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = \left(\frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2.cosh2(x)−sinh2(x)=(2ex+e−x​)2−(2ex−e−x​)2.

Expanding both squares:

=e2x+2+e−2x4−e2x−2+e−2x4=44=1.= \frac{e^{2x} + 2 + e^{-2x}}{4} - \frac{e^{2x} - 2 + e^{-2x}}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1.=4e2x+2+e−2x​−4e2x−2+e−2x​=44​=1.
What is ... the hyperbolic Pythagorean identity?

For every x∈Rx \in \mathbb{R}x∈R:

cosh⁡2(x)−sinh⁡2(x)=1.\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = 1.cosh2(x)−sinh2(x)=1.

Note the sign difference compared to the trigonometric Pythagorean identity cos⁡2(α)+sin⁡2(α)=1\cos^2(\alpha) + \sin^2(\alpha) = 1cos2(α)+sin2(α)=1.

This single sign difference, a plus in the trigonometric case and a minus in the hyperbolic case, reflects the fundamental geometric difference between the circle and the hyperbola. It also means that (cosh⁡(t),sinh⁡(t))(\cosh(t), \sinh(t))(cosh(t),sinh(t)) lies on the hyperbola x2−y2=1x^2 - y^2 = 1x2−y2=1, confirming the name.

Engineering applications¶

The catenary. When a flexible, uniform chain or cable hangs freely between two supports under its own weight, it forms a curve called the catenary. The height of the chain above its lowest point as a function of horizontal position xxx is:

y(x)=a(cosh⁡ ⁣(xa)−1),y(x) = a \left(\cosh\!\left(\frac{x}{a}\right) - 1\right),y(x)=a(cosh(ax​)−1),

where aaa is a parameter determined by the weight per unit length and the horizontal tension in the chain. Power lines between pylons, suspension bridge cables before the deck is added, and the chains of a swing set all hang in catenaries.

The catenary is often confused with a parabola because the two shapes look similar for small sag. However, they are genuinely different: a parabola describes the path of a projectile under constant gravity, while a catenary describes a hanging chain under distributed weight.

Arch bridges. An arch that carries only compressive loads with no bending, the structurally ideal form for a stone or concrete arch, has the shape of an inverted catenary. The Gateway Arch in St. Louis and many historic masonry arches follow this form.

Heat conduction and fluid flow. Hyperbolic functions appear in the analytical solution of the heat equation for fins and in certain velocity profiles in fluid mechanics. In both cases the governing differential equation has the form y′′=k2yy'' = k^2 yy′′=k2y, whose general solution is a combination of sinh⁡\sinhsinh and cosh⁡\coshcosh.

Summary and outlook¶

The hyperbolic functions sinh⁡\sinhsinh, cosh⁡\coshcosh, and tanh⁡\tanhtanh are constructed from the exponential function in a way that mirrors the structure of the trigonometric functions. The hyperbolic Pythagorean identity cosh⁡2(x)−sinh⁡2(x)=1\cosh^2(x) - \sinh^2(x) = 1cosh2(x)−sinh2(x)=1 is the counterpart of sin⁡2(x)+cos⁡2(x)=1\sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) = 1sin2(x)+cos2(x)=1, with a single crucial sign difference. The most prominent engineering application is the catenary, the shape of a hanging chain, but the functions appear wherever the differential equation y′′=k2yy'' = k^2 yy′′=k2y arises. All of the elementary functions introduced in this optional section will reappear in the chapters on differential and integral calculus.
