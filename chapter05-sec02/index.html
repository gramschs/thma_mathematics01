5.2 The Differential Quotient

In the previous section we computed the difference quotient for the parabolic marble track at x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm with three decreasing step sizes and obtained the values -0.1, -0.3, and -0.38. The numbers were converging, but we stopped short of asking where they converge to. The difference quotient measures average steepness over a finite interval. What we really want is the steepness at a single point, the instantaneous slope of the rail precisely at x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm. To find it, we must let the step size shrink all the way to zero and examine what the difference quotient approaches in that process.

  • You understand the concept of the limit of the difference quotient as h→0h \to 0h→0.

  • You know the differential quotient

    f′(x0)=lim⁡h→0f(x0+h)−f(x0)hf'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h}f′(x0​)=h→0lim​hf(x0​+h)−f(x0​)​

    as the instantaneous rate of change of fff at the point x0x_0x0​.

  • You can interpret the differential quotient geometrically as the slope of the tangent line to the graph of fff at the point (x0,f(x0))(x_0, f(x_0))(x0​,f(x0​)).

What happens as the step size approaches zero?¶

At the end of section 5.1 we observed that the secant line through P1=(5, f(5))P_1 = (5,\, f(5))P1​=(5,f(5)) and P2=(5+Δx, f(5+Δx))P_2 = (5 + \Delta x,\, f(5 + \Delta x))P2​=(5+Δx,f(5+Δx)) rotates as Δx\Delta xΔx decreases. The table below continues that observation with even smaller step sizes.

The values are clearly settling toward -0.4. They never reach it for any finite Δx\Delta xΔx, but they get arbitrarily close. In mathematics we express this by saying that the difference quotient has a limit of -0.4 as Δx\Delta xΔx approaches zero, and we write

lim⁡Δx→0f(5+Δx)−f(5)Δx=−0.4.\lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(5 + \Delta x) - f(5)}{\Delta x} = -0.4.Δx→0lim​Δxf(5+Δx)−f(5)​=−0.4.

This limiting value is the instantaneous steepness of the rail at x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm. It is not an average over any interval, no matter how short. It is the slope at a single point.

What is the differential quotient?¶

The limiting process above can be applied at any point x0x_0x0​ in the domain, not only at x0=5x_0 = 5x0​=5. For a general function fff and a general point x0x_0x0​, we define the limit of the difference quotient as the differential quotient of fff at x0x_0x0​.

What is ... the differential quotient?

Let fff be a function defined on an open interval containing x0x_0x0​. The differential quotient of fff at x0x_0x0​ is

f′(x0)=lim⁡h→0f(x0+h)−f(x0)h,f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h},f′(x0​)=h→0lim​hf(x0​+h)−f(x0​)​,

provided this limit exists. It is also called the derivative of fff at x0x_0x0​, and we read f′(x0)f'(x_0)f′(x0​) as “fff prime at x0x_0x0​”.

The differential quotient and the derivative at a point are two names for the same object. The notation f′(x0)f'(x_0)f′(x0​) was introduced by Lagrange and is the most common in engineering mathematics. An alternative notation due to Leibniz writes the same quantity as dfdx∣x0\dfrac{df}{dx}\big|_{x_0}dxdf​∣∣​x0​​, which makes the limit of the ratio Δf/Δx\Delta f / \Delta xΔf/Δx visible in the symbol itself. Both notations appear in engineering literature, and we will use them interchangeably.

How do we compute the differential quotient for the marble track?¶

We now verify the limiting value -0.4 algebraically, using the same technique that the German algebraist Carl Friedrich Gauss might have called “expanding and cancelling.” This method works for any polynomial and produces the exact limit without any guesswork.

Our height function is f(x)=0.02 x2−0.6 x+6f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6. We want to compute f′(5)f'(5)f′(5). We start from the difference quotient and expand the numerator by substituting x0+Δx=5+Δxx_0 + \Delta x = 5 + \Delta xx0​+Δx=5+Δx into the function:

f(5+Δx)=0.02 (5+Δx)2−0.6 (5+Δx)+6=0.02 (25+10 Δx+(Δx)2)−3−0.6 Δx+6=0.5+0.2 Δx+0.02 (Δx)2−3−0.6 Δx+6=3.5−0.4 Δx+0.02 (Δx)2.\begin{align*} f(5 + \Delta x) &= 0.02\,(5 + \Delta x)^2 - 0.6\,(5 + \Delta x) + 6 \\ &= 0.02\,(25 + 10\,\Delta x + (\Delta x)^2) - 3 - 0.6\,\Delta x + 6 \\ &= 0.5 + 0.2\,\Delta x + 0.02\,(\Delta x)^2 - 3 - 0.6\,\Delta x + 6 \\ &= 3.5 - 0.4\,\Delta x + 0.02\,(\Delta x)^2. \end{align*}f(5+Δx)​=0.02(5+Δx)2−0.6(5+Δx)+6=0.02(25+10Δx+(Δx)2)−3−0.6Δx+6=0.5+0.2Δx+0.02(Δx)2−3−0.6Δx+6=3.5−0.4Δx+0.02(Δx)2.​

Subtracting f(5)=3.5f(5) = 3.5f(5)=3.5 and dividing by Δx\Delta xΔx:

f(5+Δx)−f(5)Δx=3.5−0.4 Δx+0.02 (Δx)2−3.5Δx=−0.4 Δx+0.02 (Δx)2Δx=Δx (−0.4+0.02 Δx)Δx=−0.4+0.02 Δx.\begin{align*} \frac{f(5 + \Delta x) - f(5)}{\Delta x} &= \frac{3.5 - 0.4\,\Delta x + 0.02\,(\Delta x)^2 - 3.5}{\Delta x} \\ &= \frac{-0.4\,\Delta x + 0.02\,(\Delta x)^2}{\Delta x} \\ &= \frac{\Delta x\,(-0.4 + 0.02\,\Delta x)}{\Delta x} \\ &= -0.4 + 0.02\,\Delta x. \end{align*}Δxf(5+Δx)−f(5)​​=Δx3.5−0.4Δx+0.02(Δx)2−3.5​=Δx−0.4Δx+0.02(Δx)2​=ΔxΔx(−0.4+0.02Δx)​=−0.4+0.02Δx.​

We factored Δx\Delta xΔx out of the numerator and cancelled it with the Δx\Delta xΔx in the denominator. The result is a simple linear expression with no division by zero. Letting Δx→0\Delta x \to 0Δx→0 gives

f′(5)=lim⁡Δx→0(−0.4+0.02 Δx)=−0.4.f'(5) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} (-0.4 + 0.02\,\Delta x) = -0.4.f′(5)=Δx→0lim​(−0.4+0.02Δx)=−0.4.

This confirms the numerical observation from the table. At x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm the rail is descending at an instantaneous rate of 0.4 cm0.4~\text{cm}0.4 cm of height per centimetre of horizontal travel, and the negative sign tells us it is indeed descending rather than rising.

As a consistency check we can anticipate what the same calculation would give at the vertex x0=15 cmx_0 = 15~\text{cm}x0​=15 cm. The rail is neither rising nor falling at its lowest point, so we expect the instantaneous slope to be zero there. The next section derives the general formula f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6f′(x)=0.04x−0.6 for all xxx, from which f′(15)=0.04⋅15−0.6=0f'(15) = 0.04 \cdot 15 - 0.6 = 0f′(15)=0.04⋅15−0.6=0 follows immediately, confirming this expectation.

What does the differential quotient look like on the graph?¶

In section 5.1 we saw the secant line rotating as Δx\Delta xΔx decreased. The differential quotient is the slope that the secant approaches in the limit. The line with exactly that slope, passing through the point P1=(5, f(5))=(5, 3.5)P_1 = (5,\, f(5)) = (5,\, 3.5)P1​=(5,f(5))=(5,3.5), is called the tangent line to the curve at x0=5x_0 = 5x0​=5.

The tangent line touches the parabola at P1P_1P1​ and nowhere else in its immediate vicinity. Its slope, -0.4, is the best possible linear description of how the rail behaves right at that point. If we zoomed in on the graph around P1P_1P1​, magnifying more and more, the parabola would look increasingly straight, and that straight line would be the tangent. This is why the derivative is sometimes described as the slope of the curve at a point: the curve and its tangent are indistinguishable under sufficient magnification.

The full equation of the tangent line, and its role as a linear approximation of the function, will be developed in section 5.5. For now it is enough to know that the differential quotient f′(x0)f'(x_0)f′(x0​) is its slope.

Summary and outlook¶

We have defined the differential quotient as the limit of the difference quotient as the step size approaches zero. For the parabolic marble track at x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm, this limit is -0.4, a result we confirmed both numerically from a convergence table and algebraically by expanding and cancelling. Geometrically, the differential quotient is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at the chosen point. In the computation above we fixed x0=5x_0 = 5x0​=5 and obtained a single number. But the same algebraic steps work for any x0x_0x0​, and the result would be a formula in x0x_0x0​ rather than a single value. That formula is called the derivative function, and constructing it is the subject of the next section.
