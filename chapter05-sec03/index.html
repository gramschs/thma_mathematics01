5.3 The Derivative Function

In section 5.2 we computed the differential quotient of the parabolic marble track at the single point x0=5 cmx_0 = 5~\text{cm}x0​=5 cm and found f′(5)=−0.4f'(5) = -0.4f′(5)=−0.4. The closing observation was that the same algebraic steps would work for any choice of x0x_0x0​, and the result would be a formula rather than a single number. We now carry out that calculation. The outcome is a new function that assigns to every position on the track the instantaneous slope at that position, a complete map of the track’s steepness.

Learning goals

  • You know the definition of the derivative function

    f′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)h.f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}.f′(x)=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​.

  • You are familiar with the standard notations for the derivative: f′(x)f'(x)f′(x), dfdx\dfrac{df}{dx}dxdf​, and f˙(t)\dot{f}(t)f˙​(t).

  • You can compute the derivative function of a polynomial directly from the limit definition.

From one point to the whole domain¶

In section 5.2 we fixed x0=5x_0 = 5x0​=5 before expanding f(x0+h)f(x_0 + h)f(x0​+h). The number 5 appeared repeatedly in the algebra, but it played no special role: every step would have worked identically with any other value. The only thing that changes when we replace 5 by a general variable xxx is that the final answer is no longer a number but an expression in xxx.

We set up the difference quotient for our height function f(x)=0.02 x2−0.6 x+6f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6 at a general point xxx:

f(x+h)=0.02 (x+h)2−0.6 (x+h)+6=0.02 (x2+2xh+h2)−0.6 x−0.6 h+6=0.02 x2+0.04 xh+0.02 h2−0.6 x−0.6 h+6.\begin{align*} f(x + h) &= 0.02\,(x + h)^2 - 0.6\,(x + h) + 6 \\ &= 0.02\,(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - 0.6\,x - 0.6\,h + 6 \\ &= 0.02\,x^2 + 0.04\,x h + 0.02\,h^2 - 0.6\,x - 0.6\,h + 6. \end{align*}f(x+h)​=0.02(x+h)2−0.6(x+h)+6=0.02(x2+2xh+h2)−0.6x−0.6h+6=0.02x2+0.04xh+0.02h2−0.6x−0.6h+6.​

Subtracting f(x)=0.02 x2−0.6 x+6f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6, the terms that do not involve hhh cancel completely:

f(x+h)−f(x)=0.04 xh+0.02 h2−0.6 h=h (0.04 x−0.6+0.02 h).\begin{align*} f(x + h) - f(x) &= 0.04\,xh + 0.02\,h^2 - 0.6\,h \\ &= h\,(0.04\,x - 0.6 + 0.02\,h). \end{align*}f(x+h)−f(x)​=0.04xh+0.02h2−0.6h=h(0.04x−0.6+0.02h).​

Dividing by hhh and taking the limit:

f′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)h=lim⁡h→0h (0.04 x−0.6+0.02 h)h=lim⁡h→0(0.04 x−0.6+0.02 h)=0.04 x−0.6.\begin{align*} f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h\,(0.04\,x - 0.6 + 0.02\,h)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} (0.04\,x - 0.6 + 0.02\,h) = 0.04\,x - 0.6. \end{align*}f′(x)​=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​=h→0lim​hh(0.04x−0.6+0.02h)​=h→0lim​(0.04x−0.6+0.02h)=0.04x−0.6.​

The derivative function of our parabolic track is

f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6,x∈[0,30].f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6, \quad x \in [0, 30].f′(x)=0.04x−0.6,x∈[0,30].

This is a linear function. The derivative of a polynomial of degree 2 is a polynomial of degree 1 — one degree lower. We will see in the next chapter that this pattern holds for every power function.

We can immediately verify the earlier result: substituting x=5x = 5x=5 gives f′(5)=0.04⋅5−0.6=0.2−0.6=−0.4f'(5) = 0.04 \cdot 5 - 0.6 = 0.2 - 0.6 = -0.4f′(5)=0.04⋅5−0.6=0.2−0.6=−0.4, which agrees exactly with the value computed by the longer route in section 5.2.

What is the derivative function?¶

The procedure above can be applied to any function fff, not only to our specific parabola. If the limit of the difference quotient exists at every point of the domain, the assignment x↦f′(x)x \mapsto f'(x)x↦f′(x) defines a new function.

What is ... the derivative function?

Let fff be defined on an open interval III. If the limit

f′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)hf'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}f′(x)=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​

exists for every x∈Ix \in Ix∈I, then the function f′:I→Rf': I \to \mathbb{R}f′:I→R, x↦f′(x)x \mapsto f'(x)x↦f′(x), is called the derivative function of fff. The process of computing f′f'f′ from fff is called differentiation.

A few points are worth noting. First, f′f'f′ is a genuine function in its own right, with its own domain, range, zeros, and graph. Second, the domain of f′f'f′ can be smaller than the domain of fff: if the limit fails to exist at some point, that point is excluded from the domain of f′f'f′. We examine exactly this possibility in section 5.4. Third, differentiating f′f'f′ a second time produces the second derivative f′′f''f′′, which measures how rapidly the slope itself is changing. These higher derivatives will appear naturally in later chapters when we study approximation and optimization.

What does the derivative function reveal about the track?¶

We now have a formula f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6f′(x)=0.04x−0.6 that we can evaluate at every position along the track. The following table records the slope at several points of interest.

Position xxx [cm]f′(x)f'(x)f′(x)Interpretation
0-0.6steepest descent, at the starting block
5-0.4falling, confirms section 5.2
150horizontal, at the lowest point
25+0.4rising, symmetric counterpart of x=5x = 5x=5

Three observations stand out. The derivative is zero at x=15x = 15x=15, the vertex of the parabola. This is not a coincidence: the vertex is the point where the track transitions from descending to ascending, so its instantaneous slope must be zero there. We will make this observation the foundation of optimization in a later chapter, where finding zeros of the derivative is the key technique for locating minima and maxima of any differentiable function.

The derivative is negative for x<15x < 15x<15 and positive for x>15x > 15x>15. Comparing this with the definition of monotonicity from section 3.2, we see that the derivative gives us a precise algebraic criterion for what we described there geometrically: a function is increasing on an interval where f′(x)>0f'(x) > 0f′(x)>0 and decreasing where f′(x)<0f'(x) < 0f′(x)<0.

The values at x=5x = 5x=5 and x=25x = 25x=25 are equal in magnitude and opposite in sign: f′(5)=−0.4f'(5) = -0.4f′(5)=−0.4 and f′(25)=+0.4f'(25) = +0.4f′(25)=+0.4. The same holds for x=0x = 0x=0 and x=30x = 30x=30. This reflects the symmetry of the parabola about its axis at x=15x = 15x=15: the track rises on the right at the same rate at which it falls on the left.

How is the derivative written?¶

The derivative function f′f'f′ can be written in several equivalent notations. All three appear in engineering literature and textbooks, and it is important to recognise each of them.

The Lagrange notation f′(x)f'(x)f′(x) is read as “fff prime of xxx”. It is concise and is the standard choice in this course. The derivative at a specific point x0x_0x0​ is written f′(x0)f'(x_0)f′(x0​).

The Leibniz notation dfdx\dfrac{df}{dx}dxdf​ is read as “dfdfdf by dxdxdx” or “the derivative of fff with respect to xxx”. It makes the limit of the ratio Δf/Δx\Delta f / \Delta xΔf/Δx visible in the symbol itself and is particularly expressive when we want to name the variable of differentiation explicitly. The evaluated form dfdx∣x0\dfrac{df}{dx}\big|_{x_0}dxdf​∣∣​x0​​ gives the derivative at the specific point x0x_0x0​. Leibniz notation is especially common in physics and in the formulation of differential equations.

The Newton notation f˙(t)\dot{f}(t)f˙​(t) is read as “fff dot”. It is used exclusively when the independent variable is time ttt. In mechanical engineering, if s(t)s(t)s(t) is the position of a component as a function of time, then s˙(t)\dot{s}(t)s˙(t) is its velocity and s¨(t)\ddot{s}(t)s¨(t) is its acceleration. The dot notation is compact and is standard in dynamics, control engineering, and any field where time derivatives appear frequently.

All three notations refer to the same mathematical object. For our marble track, where the independent variable is position xxx, we use Lagrange or Leibniz notation:

f′(x)=dfdx=0.04 x−0.6.f'(x) = \frac{df}{dx} = 0.04\,x - 0.6.f′(x)=dxdf​=0.04x−0.6.

If instead we imagined the marble moving along the track and described its height as a function of time ttt, we would write f˙(t)\dot{f}(t)f˙​(t) for the rate of change of height with respect to time.

Summary and outlook¶

We have extended the differential quotient from a number at one point to a function defined across the entire domain. For the parabolic marble track, the limit calculation at a general point xxx produces the derivative function f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6f′(x)=0.04x−0.6, a linear function that encodes the instantaneous slope of the track at every position. The zero of f′f'f′ at x=15x = 15x=15 marks the vertex, the negative values for x<15x < 15x<15 confirm the descending left half, and the positive values for x>15x > 15x>15 confirm the ascending right half. The calculation we performed assumed without question that the limit exists at every point of the domain. For our smooth parabola this is true, but it need not be true in general. A track with a sharp kink, for instance, has no well-defined tangent direction at the kink, and therefore no derivative there. Understanding exactly when a function is differentiable, and what goes wrong when it is not, is the subject of the next section.
