6.1 Derivatives of Elementary Functions

In chapter 5 we computed derivatives from the limit definition, expanding and cancelling step by step. For the parabolic marble track that calculation took the better part of a page of algebra. That approach is rigorous and illuminating, but repeating it from scratch every time we encounter a new function would make practical work impossibly slow. The solution is to carry out the derivation once for each family of elementary functions and collect the results in a reference table. In this section we build that table for the functions introduced in chapters 3 and 4: power functions, the exponential function, the natural logarithm, and the trigonometric functions.

Learning goals

  • You know the derivative of power functions: (xn)′=n xn−1(x^n)' = n\,x^{n-1}(xn)′=nxn−1 for n∈Rn \in \mathbb{R}n∈R.

  • You know the derivatives of the exponential function (ex)′=ex(e^x)' = e^x(ex)′=ex and the natural logarithm (ln⁡x)′=1x(\ln x)' = \dfrac{1}{x}(lnx)′=x1​.

  • You know the derivatives of the trigonometric functions (sin⁡x)′=cos⁡x(\sin x)' = \cos x(sinx)′=cosx, (cos⁡x)′=−sin⁡x(\cos x)' = -\sin x(cosx)′=−sinx, and (tan⁡x)′=1cos⁡2x(\tan x)' = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x}(tanx)′=cos2x1​.

What does the marble track already tell us?¶

In section 5.3 we applied the limit definition to the parabolic track height f(x)=0.02 x2−0.6 x+6f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6 at a general point xxx and found

f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6.f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6.f′(x)=0.04x−0.6.

Looking at how each term transformed, a pattern emerges. The term 0.02 x20.02\,x^20.02x2 became 0.04 x0.04\,x0.04x: the exponent 2 descended as a multiplicative factor and the exponent decreased by one. The term 0.6 x=0.6 x10.6\,x = 0.6\,x^10.6x=0.6x1 became 0.6: the same pattern with exponent 1. The constant 6=6 x06 = 6\,x^06=6x0 vanished: multiplying by the exponent 0 kills the term. Is this a general rule, or a coincidence of this particular function?

What is the power rule?¶

It is a general rule. To see why, we apply the limit definition to f(x)=xnf(x) = x^nf(x)=xn for a positive integer nnn. When we expand (x+h)n(x + h)^n(x+h)n, the result is a sum that starts with xnx^nxn, followed by a term n xn−1 hn\,x^{n-1}\,hnxn−1h, and then terms that all contain h2h^2h2 or higher powers of hhh:

(x+h)n=xn+n xn−1 h+(terms with h2,h3,…,hn).(x + h)^n = x^n + n\,x^{n-1}\,h + (\text{terms with } h^2, h^3, \ldots, h^n).(x+h)n=xn+nxn−1h+(terms with h2,h3,…,hn).

Subtracting xnx^nxn, dividing by hhh, and letting h→0h \to 0h→0, every term containing a remaining factor of hhh vanishes and we are left with

(xn)′=n xn−1.(x^n)' = n\,x^{n-1}.(xn)′=nxn−1.
What is ... the power rule?

For any real exponent n∈Rn \in \mathbb{R}n∈R:

(xn)′=n xn−1.\bigl(x^n\bigr)' = n\,x^{n-1}.(xn)′=nxn−1.

The rule extends to all real exponents, not only positive integers. For n=−1n = -1n=−1 we obtain (x−1)′=−x−2(x^{-1})' = -x^{-2}(x−1)′=−x−2, and for n=12n = \tfrac{1}{2}n=21​ we obtain (x )′=12 x−1/2(\sqrt{x}\,)' = \tfrac{1}{2}\,x^{-1/2}(x​)′=21​x−1/2. The extension to arbitrary real exponents uses the rewriting xn=enln⁡xx^n = e^{n \ln x}xn=enlnx (valid for x>0x > 0x>0), combined with the derivatives of exe^xex and ln⁡x\ln xlnx and the chain rule, tools we develop in sections 6.4 and 6.5.

Returning to our marble track: applying the power rule to each term of f(x)=0.02 x2−0.6 x+6f(x) = 0.02\,x^2 - 0.6\,x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6 recovers f′(x)=0.04 x−0.6f'(x) = 0.04\,x - 0.6f′(x)=0.04x−0.6 immediately. The same result that required a full page in section 5.3 now follows in a single line. The rules for combining terms systematically are formalised in section 6.2.

What are the derivatives of the exponential function and the natural logarithm?¶

In section 4.1 we introduced Euler’s number through its defining property: exe^xex is the only exponential function whose rate of change at every point equals its own value at that point. Translated directly into derivative notation, this reads

(ex)′=ex.(e^x)' = e^x.(ex)′=ex.

No limit calculation is required. The equation is not a result we derive from scratch; it is a direct translation of what makes eee special in the first place.

The natural logarithm ln⁡x\ln xlnx is the inverse of exe^xex, and its derivative is

(ln⁡x)′=1x,x>0.(\ln x)' = \frac{1}{x}, \quad x > 0.(lnx)′=x1​,x>0.

We include this result in the reference table below. The derivation belongs properly to section 6.5, where the inverse function rule makes it a one-line consequence of (ex)′=ex(e^x)' = e^x(ex)′=ex.

What are the derivatives of the trigonometric functions?¶

For the sine function, expanding sin⁡(x+h)\sin(x + h)sin(x+h) with the addition theorem from section 4.5 and applying the standard limits lim⁡h→0sin⁡hh=1\lim_{h \to 0}\tfrac{\sin h}{h} = 1limh→0​hsinh​=1 and lim⁡h→0cos⁡h−1h=0\lim_{h \to 0}\tfrac{\cos h - 1}{h} = 0limh→0​hcosh−1​=0 gives

(sin⁡x)′=cos⁡x.(\sin x)' = \cos x.(sinx)′=cosx.

The same procedure applied to cosine yields

(cos⁡x)′=−sin⁡x.(\cos x)' = -\sin x.(cosx)′=−sinx.

The negative sign has a direct geometric meaning: (cos⁡x)′=−sin⁡x(\cos x)' = -\sin x(cosx)′=−sinx means that cosine is increasing where sine is negative, and decreasing where sine is positive. Equivalently, the extrema of cosine coincide exactly with the zeros of sine, and vice versa. On the unit circle the two functions are always a quarter period out of phase, and differentiation reflects this relationship exactly.

The derivative of the tangent follows from the quotient rule, which we develop in section 6.3. The result is

(tan⁡x)′=1cos⁡2x,(\tan x)' = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x},(tanx)′=cos2x1​,

valid wherever cos⁡x≠0\cos x \neq 0cosx=0.

These are not only abstract formulas. The position of a mass in a vibrating mechanical system is often a product of an exponential decay and a trigonometric function. Computing the velocity of that mass requires differentiating exactly this kind of expression, and we will work through it in detail starting from section 6.3.

Summary and outlook¶

We have established the derivatives of all the elementary function families from chapters 3 and 4. The power rule unifies the polynomial results from chapter 5: every monomial xnx^nxn differentiates by bringing the exponent down as a factor and reducing it by one, recovering in a single step the derivative of the marble track height that cost a full page of algebra in section 5.3. The exponential function exe^xex is its own derivative, a property that singles out Euler’s number as the natural base for calculus. Sine and cosine differentiate into each other with a sign change that reflects their phase relationship on the unit circle. The derivatives of the tangent and the natural logarithm are stated here for the reference table and derived properly in sections 6.3 and 6.5 respectively. With this table in hand, the next question is how to combine results: the following section introduces the rules for differentiating sums and scalar multiples of functions, completing the toolkit for any linear combination of elementary functions.
