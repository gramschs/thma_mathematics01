6.3 Product Rule and Quotient Rule

The rules of section 6.2 allow us to differentiate any sum or scalar multiple of elementary functions, and together with the power rule they make every polynomial routine. But functions in mechanical engineering are rarely just sums. The position of a damped oscillator, for example, is a product of an exponential decay and a cosine oscillation: two non-trivial functions multiplied together. No combination of the sum and factor rules can handle such an expression. We need a rule for products, and a companion rule for quotients.

Learning goals

  • You know the product rule

    (f⋅g)′=f′⋅g+f⋅g′\bigl(f \cdot g\bigr)' = f' \cdot g + f \cdot g'(f⋅g)′=f′⋅g+f⋅g′

    and can apply it to products of elementary functions.

  • You know the quotient rule

    (fg)′=f′⋅g−f⋅g′g2\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)' = \dfrac{f' \cdot g - f \cdot g'}{g^2}(gf​)′=g2f′⋅g−f⋅g′​

    and can apply it wherever g≠0g \neq 0g=0.

  • You can identify when the product or quotient rule is needed and carry out the differentiation correctly, including simplification of the result.

How does a damped oscillator move?¶

A mass attached to a spring and a viscous damper is one of the most fundamental models in mechanical engineering. The spring pulls the mass back toward equilibrium with a force proportional to the displacement; the damper resists motion with a force proportional to the velocity and converts kinetic energy into heat. When the mass is displaced and released, it does not oscillate indefinitely: the amplitude shrinks with every cycle as the damper dissipates energy.

What is the product rule?¶

We apply the limit definition directly to the product f(x)⋅g(x)f(x)\cdot g(x)f(x)⋅g(x). The standard approach is to add and subtract a bridge term f(x) g(x+h)f(x)\,g(x+h)f(x)g(x+h) in the numerator, which allows the difference quotient to be split into two recognisable pieces:

(f⋅g)′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h) g(x+h)−f(x) g(x)h=lim⁡h→0f(x+h) g(x+h)−f(x) g(x+h)+f(x) g(x+h)−f(x) g(x)h=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)h⋅g(x+h)  +  f(x)⋅lim⁡h→0g(x+h)−g(x)h.\begin{align*} \bigl(f \cdot g\bigr)'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)\,g(x+h) - f(x)\,g(x)}{h} \\[4pt] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)\,g(x+h) - f(x)\,g(x+h) + f(x)\,g(x+h) - f(x)\,g(x)}{h} \\[4pt] &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\cdot g(x+h) \;+\; f(x)\cdot \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h}. \end{align*}(f⋅g)′(x)​=h→0lim​hf(x+h)g(x+h)−f(x)g(x)​=h→0lim​hf(x+h)g(x+h)−f(x)g(x+h)+f(x)g(x+h)−f(x)g(x)​=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​⋅g(x+h)+f(x)⋅h→0lim​hg(x+h)−g(x)​.​

As h→0h \to 0h→0, the first ratio approaches f′(x)f'(x)f′(x) and g(x+h)g(x + h)g(x+h) approaches g(x)g(x)g(x) by continuity. The second ratio approaches g′(x)g'(x)g′(x). We arrive at

(f⋅g)′=f′⋅g+f⋅g′.\bigl(f \cdot g\bigr)' = f' \cdot g + f \cdot g'.(f⋅g)′=f′⋅g+f⋅g′.
What is ... the product rule?

For any two differentiable functions fff and ggg:

(f(x)⋅g(x))′=f′(x)⋅g(x)+f(x)⋅g′(x).\bigl(f(x) \cdot g(x)\bigr)' = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x).(f(x)⋅g(x))′=f′(x)⋅g(x)+f(x)⋅g′(x).

A useful way to read this rule aloud is: derivative of the first times the second, plus the first times the derivative of the second. The two terms capture the independent contribution of each factor to the overall rate of change.

Notice that the rule is not symmetric: (f⋅g)′≠f′⋅g′(f \cdot g)' \neq f' \cdot g'(f⋅g)′=f′⋅g′. The product of the derivatives is not the derivative of the product, just as the composition of two functions is not the composition of their derivatives, as we discussed in section 3.6.

What is the velocity of the damped oscillator?¶

We apply the product rule to x(t)=A e−δtcos⁡(ωd t)x(t) = A\,e^{-\delta t}\cos(\omega_d\,t)x(t)=Ae−δtcos(ωd​t), identifying f(t)=A e−δtf(t) = A\,e^{-\delta t}f(t)=Ae−δt and g(t)=cos⁡(ωd t)g(t) = \cos(\omega_d\,t)g(t)=cos(ωd​t).

v(t)=x′(t)=f′(t) g(t)+f(t) g′(t)=−Aδ e−δtcos⁡(ωd t)−Aωd e−δtsin⁡(ωd t)=−A e−δt(δcos⁡(ωd t)+ωdsin⁡(ωd t)).\begin{align*} v(t) = x'(t) &= f'(t)\,g(t) + f(t)\,g'(t) \\ &= -A\delta\,e^{-\delta t}\cos(\omega_d\,t) - A\omega_d\,e^{-\delta t}\sin(\omega_d\,t) \\ &= -A\,e^{-\delta t}\bigl(\delta\cos(\omega_d\,t) + \omega_d\sin(\omega_d\,t)\bigr). \end{align*}v(t)=x′(t)​=f′(t)g(t)+f(t)g′(t)=−Aδe−δtcos(ωd​t)−Aωd​e−δtsin(ωd​t)=−Ae−δt(δcos(ωd​t)+ωd​sin(ωd​t)).​

What is the quotient rule?¶

Products and quotients are closely related, and the quotient rule can be derived directly from the product rule without returning to the limit definition. Let q(x)=f(x)/g(x)q(x) = f(x)/g(x)q(x)=f(x)/g(x), so that f(x)=q(x)⋅g(x)f(x) = q(x)\cdot g(x)f(x)=q(x)⋅g(x). Differentiating both sides with the product rule:

f′(x)=q′(x)⋅g(x)+q(x)⋅g′(x).f'(x) = q'(x)\cdot g(x) + q(x)\cdot g'(x).f′(x)=q′(x)⋅g(x)+q(x)⋅g′(x).

Solving for q′(x)q'(x)q′(x) and substituting q(x)=f(x)/g(x)q(x) = f(x)/g(x)q(x)=f(x)/g(x):

q′(x)=f′(x)−q(x)⋅g′(x)g(x)=f′(x)−f(x)g(x)⋅g′(x)g(x)=f′(x) g(x)−f(x) g′(x)g(x)2.q'(x) = \frac{f'(x) - q(x)\cdot g'(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(x) - \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\cdot g'(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{g(x)^2}.q′(x)=g(x)f′(x)−q(x)⋅g′(x)​=g(x)f′(x)−g(x)f(x)​⋅g′(x)​=g(x)2f′(x)g(x)−f(x)g′(x)​.
What is ... the quotient rule?

For two differentiable functions fff and ggg with g(x)≠0g(x) \neq 0g(x)=0:

(f(x)g(x))′=f′(x) g(x)−f(x) g′(x)g(x)2.\left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right)' = \frac{f'(x)\,g(x) - f(x)\,g'(x)}{g(x)^2}.(g(x)f(x)​)′=g(x)2f′(x)g(x)−f(x)g′(x)​.

The order of terms in the numerator matters: it is always f′gf'gf′g minus fg′f g'fg′, never the other way around. A sign error here produces a result that is wrong by a sign and a factor of two, so it is worth double-checking by substituting a simple example.

Can we verify the derivative of the tangent?¶

In section 6.1 we stated without proof that (tan⁡x)′=1/cos⁡2x(\tan x)' = 1/\cos^2 x(tanx)′=1/cos2x. The quotient rule now lets us derive this result. We write tan⁡x=sin⁡x/cos⁡x\tan x = \sin x / \cos xtanx=sinx/cosx and identify f(x)=sin⁡xf(x) = \sin xf(x)=sinx and g(x)=cos⁡xg(x) = \cos xg(x)=cosx, so f′(x)=cos⁡xf'(x) = \cos xf′(x)=cosx and g′(x)=−sin⁡xg'(x) = -\sin xg′(x)=−sinx. Applying the quotient rule:

(tan⁡x)′=cos⁡x⋅cos⁡x−sin⁡x⋅(−sin⁡x)cos⁡2x=cos⁡2x+sin⁡2xcos⁡2x.(\tan x)' = \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x}.(tanx)′=cos2xcosx⋅cosx−sinx⋅(−sinx)​=cos2xcos2x+sin2x​.

The numerator is exactly the Pythagorean identity from section 4.5: cos⁡2x+sin⁡2x=1\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x = 1cos2x+sin2x=1. Therefore

(tan⁡x)′=1cos⁡2x,(\tan x)' = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x},(tanx)′=cos2x1​,

confirming the entry in the reference table of section 6.1. The Pythagorean identity, which we proved geometrically from the unit circle, turns out to play an algebraic role in differential calculus as well.

Summary and outlook¶

The product rule and the quotient rule complete the toolkit for differentiating algebraic combinations of functions. Applied to the damped oscillator, the product rule produces the velocity in a direct calculation, and the result decays to zero as expected from the physical model. The quotient rule, derived from the product rule without a separate limit argument, closes the open item from section 6.1 by giving a clean derivation of (tan⁡x)′=1/cos⁡2x(\tan x)' = 1/\cos^2 x(tanx)′=1/cos2x. Both rules required knowing the derivatives of the individual factors, and in the oscillator example those factors were composite functions whose derivatives we used without yet justifying them. Providing that justification is the task of the next section, where the chain rule handles any composition of differentiable functions.
