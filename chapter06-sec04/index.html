6.4 The Chain Rule

In section 6.3 we computed the velocity of the damped oscillator and obtained a correct and physically meaningful result. But we relied on two derivative formulas that we used without proof: (e−δt)′=−δ e−δt(e^{-\delta t})' = -\delta\,e^{-\delta t}(e−δt)′=−δe−δt and (cos⁡(ωdt))′=−ωdsin⁡(ωdt)(\cos(\omega_d t))' = -\omega_d\sin(\omega_d t)(cos(ωd​t))′=−ωd​sin(ωd​t). Neither of these follows from anything in sections 6.1 or 6.2, because both expressions are composite functions: an elementary function applied not to the variable ttt directly but to a linear function of ttt. Differentiating composite functions is the task of the chain rule, and it is the most frequently applied differentiation rule in engineering practice.

Learning goals

  • You know the chain rule: if h(x)=f(g(x))h(x) = f(g(x))h(x)=f(g(x)), then

    h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x).h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).

  • You can identify the inner function ggg and the outer function fff in a composite expression, as practiced in section 3.6.

  • You can apply the chain rule to differentiate composite functions involving exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, and power functions.

How does a composition change when its input changes?¶

In section 3.6 we defined the composition h(x)=f(g(x))h(x) = f(g(x))h(x)=f(g(x)) and practiced identifying the inner function ggg and the outer function fff. The key question was: what is the last operation performed? That last operation is the outer function, and everything inside it is the inner function.

We now ask a calculus question about the same construction: if the input xxx changes by a small amount Δx\Delta xΔx, by how much does the output h(x)h(x)h(x) change? The inner function ggg responds first, changing by approximately g′(x) Δxg'(x)\,\Delta xg′(x)Δx. The outer function fff then responds to this intermediate change, scaling it by f′(g(x))f'(g(x))f′(g(x)). The overall change in hhh is approximately the product of the two responses:

Δh≈f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)⋅Δx.\Delta h \approx f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \cdot \Delta x.Δh≈f′(g(x))⋅g′(x)⋅Δx.

Dividing by Δx\Delta xΔx and passing to the limit gives the chain rule.

What is ... the chain rule?

Let ggg be differentiable at xxx and fff be differentiable at g(x)g(x)g(x). Then the composition h(x)=f(g(x))h(x) = f(g(x))h(x)=f(g(x)) is differentiable at xxx and

h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).h'(x) = f'\bigl(g(x)\bigr) \cdot g'(x).h′(x)=f′(g(x))⋅g′(x).

In words: the derivative of the outer function evaluated at the inner function, multiplied by the derivative of the inner function.

The Leibniz notation makes the chain rule particularly transparent. Writing u=g(x)u = g(x)u=g(x) and y=f(u)=h(x)y = f(u) = h(x)y=f(u)=h(x), the chain rule reads

dydx=dydu⋅dudx.\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}.dxdy​=dudy​⋅dxdu​.

The intermediate variable uuu appears to cancel between numerator and denominator, just as it would in ordinary fractions. This is a mnemonic, not a proof, but it is a reliable one and matches how engineers typically reason about rates of change through a chain of dependencies.

How do we differentiate the oscillator’s exponential factor?¶

We return to the decaying exponential f(t)=A e−δtf(t) = A\,e^{-\delta t}f(t)=Ae−δt from section 6.3. The outer function is eue^ueu and the inner function is u=−δtu = -\delta tu=−δt. From the reference table in section 6.1 we know (eu)′=eu(e^u)' = e^u(eu)′=eu, and the inner function has derivative (−δt)′=−δ(-\delta t)' = -\delta(−δt)′=−δ. Applying the chain rule:

(A e−δt)′=A⋅e−δt⋅(−δ)=−Aδ e−δt.\bigl(A\,e^{-\delta t}\bigr)' = A \cdot e^{-\delta t} \cdot (-\delta) = -A\delta\,e^{-\delta t}.(Ae−δt)′=A⋅e−δt⋅(−δ)=−Aδe−δt.

This confirms the result we used without proof in section 6.3. The physical interpretation is immediate: the rate at which the envelope decays is proportional to its current value, with δ\deltaδ as the constant of proportionality. A larger damping exponent δ\deltaδ means a faster collapse of the amplitude, and the derivative reflects this linearly.

As a consistency check, we evaluate at t=0t = 0t=0 with A=0.10 mA = 0.10~\text{m}A=0.10 m and δ=0.5 s−1\delta = 0.5~\text{s}^{-1}δ=0.5 s−1:

(A e−δt)′∣t=0=−0.10⋅0.5⋅e0=−0.05 ms.\bigl(A\,e^{-\delta t}\bigr)'\big|_{t=0} = -0.10 \cdot 0.5 \cdot e^{0} = -0.05~\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}.(Ae−δt)′∣∣​t=0​=−0.10⋅0.5⋅e0=−0.05 sm​.

At the moment of release the envelope is shrinking at 0.05 m/s0.05~\text{m/s}0.05 m/s, which matches the initial velocity we computed in section 6.3.

How do we differentiate the oscillator’s cosine factor?¶

The cosine factor g(t)=cos⁡(ωd t)g(t) = \cos(\omega_d\,t)g(t)=cos(ωd​t) is the outer function cos⁡(u)\cos(u)cos(u) applied to the inner function u=ωd tu = \omega_d\,tu=ωd​t. From section 6.1, (cos⁡u)′=−sin⁡u(\cos u)' = -\sin u(cosu)′=−sinu, and the inner function has derivative ωd\omega_dωd​. The chain rule gives:

(cos⁡(ωd t))′=−sin⁡(ωd t)⋅ωd=−ωdsin⁡(ωd t).\bigl(\cos(\omega_d\,t)\bigr)' = -\sin(\omega_d\,t) \cdot \omega_d = -\omega_d\sin(\omega_d\,t).(cos(ωd​t))′=−sin(ωd​t)⋅ωd​=−ωd​sin(ωd​t).

This also confirms the result used in section 6.3. The factor ωd\omega_dωd​ in front of the sine is physically the angular frequency: a higher frequency means faster oscillation, and the derivative is correspondingly larger in magnitude.

We can verify at t=0t = 0t=0 with ωd=3 rad/s\omega_d = 3~\text{rad/s}ωd​=3 rad/s:

(cos⁡(ωd t))′∣t=0=−3sin⁡(0)=0.\bigl(\cos(\omega_d\,t)\bigr)'\big|_{t=0} = -3\sin(0) = 0.(cos(ωd​t))′∣∣​t=0​=−3sin(0)=0.

At t=0t = 0t=0 the cosine is at its maximum value of 1 and has zero slope, consistent with the flat peak of the cosine graph at the origin.

How do we handle more complex compositions?¶

The chain rule applies whenever one function is nested inside another, regardless of how many layers there are. The process is always the same: identify the outermost operation, differentiate it while treating the inner expression as a single unit, then multiply by the derivative of that inner expression.

Consider h(x)=sin⁡(x2)h(x) = \sin(x^2)h(x)=sin(x2). The outer function is sin⁡(u)\sin(u)sin(u) and the inner function is u=x2u = x^2u=x2. Applying the chain rule:

h′(x)=cos⁡(x2)⋅2x.h'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x.h′(x)=cos(x2)⋅2x.

Consider h(x)=esin⁡xh(x) = e^{\sin x}h(x)=esinx. The outer function is eue^ueu and the inner function is u=sin⁡xu = \sin xu=sinx. Applying the chain rule:

h′(x)=esin⁡x⋅cos⁡x.h'(x) = e^{\sin x} \cdot \cos x.h′(x)=esinx⋅cosx.

Consider h(x)=1−x2h(x) = \sqrt{1 - x^2}h(x)=1−x2​, which we recognised as a composition in section 3.6. Writing it as (1−x2)1/2(1 - x^2)^{1/2}(1−x2)1/2, the outer function is u1/2u^{1/2}u1/2 and the inner function is u=1−x2u = 1 - x^2u=1−x2. The chain rule and the power rule give:

h′(x)=12(1−x2)−1/2⋅(−2x)=−x1−x2.h'(x) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - x^2)^{-1/2} \cdot (-2x) = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}.h′(x)=21​(1−x2)−1/2⋅(−2x)=1−x2​−x​.

This last example also completes the loose end from section 6.1, where we noted that the power rule extends to real exponents but deferred the argument. For any f(x)=xrf(x) = x^rf(x)=xr with real rrr and x>0x > 0x>0, writing xr=erln⁡xx^r = e^{r\ln x}xr=erlnx and applying the chain rule gives (xr)′=erln⁡x⋅r/x=r xr−1(x^r)' = e^{r\ln x} \cdot r/x = r\,x^{r-1}(xr)′=erlnx⋅r/x=rxr−1, confirming the power rule for all real exponents.

Summary and outlook¶

The chain rule closes the justification gap left open in section 6.3: the two derivative formulas used there for the exponential and cosine factors of the damped oscillator both follow in a single application of the rule. The Leibniz form dy/dx=(dy/du)(du/dx)dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx)dy/dx=(dy/du)(du/dx) provides an intuitive reading in terms of rates propagating through a chain of functions. The chain rule also extends the power rule to all real exponents, completing the statement made in section 6.1. With the product rule, the quotient rule, and the chain rule all in place, virtually every function built from the elementary families of chapters 3 and 4 is now differentiable in a finite number of steps. One family of functions requires a slightly different approach: the inverse functions introduced in section 4.2, whose derivatives follow from the chain rule by a short and elegant argument. That argument is the subject of the next section.
