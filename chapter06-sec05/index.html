6.5 The Inverse Function Rule

In section 4.2 we constructed inverse functions by reflecting graphs about the line y=xy = xy=x, and we noted that domain and range swap in the process. We now ask the calculus question that naturally follows: if we know the derivative of a function, what can we say about the derivative of its inverse? The answer follows from the chain rule in a few lines and gives us, in one unified step, the derivative of the natural logarithm and the derivatives of the inverse trigonometric functions.

Learning goals

  • You know the inverse function rule: if fff is differentiable and invertible near x0x_0x0​, then

    (f−1)′(y0)=1f′(f−1(y0)).\bigl(f^{-1}\bigr)'(y_0) = \frac{1}{f'\bigl(f^{-1}(y_0)\bigr)}.(f−1)′(y0​)=f′(f−1(y0​))1​.

  • You can use the inverse function rule to derive the derivatives of ln⁡(x)\ln(x)ln(x), arcsin⁡(x)\arcsin(x)arcsin(x), arccos⁡(x)\arccos(x)arccos(x), and arctan⁡(x)\arctan(x)arctan(x).

  • You are familiar with the inverse trigonometric functions arcsin⁡\arcsinarcsin, arccos⁡\arccosarccos, and arctan⁡\arctanarctan, their domains, and their ranges.

Where does the rule come from?¶

In section 4.2 we established that fff and its inverse f−1f^{-1}f−1 undo each other completely. Written as a composition, this means

f(f−1(y))=yf\bigl(f^{-1}(y)\bigr) = yf(f−1(y))=y

for all yyy in the domain of f−1f^{-1}f−1. The right-hand side is the identity function, whose derivative is 1. We differentiate the left-hand side using the chain rule from section 6.4, treating f−1(y)f^{-1}(y)f−1(y) as the inner function and fff as the outer function:

f′(f−1(y))⋅(f−1)′(y)=1.f'\bigl(f^{-1}(y)\bigr) \cdot \bigl(f^{-1}\bigr)'(y) = 1.f′(f−1(y))⋅(f−1)′(y)=1.

Solving for (f−1)′(y)(f^{-1})'(y)(f−1)′(y), provided f′(f−1(y))≠0f'(f^{-1}(y)) \neq 0f′(f−1(y))=0:

(f−1)′(y)=1f′(f−1(y)).\bigl(f^{-1}\bigr)'(y) = \frac{1}{f'\bigl(f^{-1}(y)\bigr)}.(f−1)′(y)=f′(f−1(y))1​.
What is ... the inverse function rule?

Let fff be differentiable and invertible on an open interval, and suppose f′(x)≠0f'(x) \neq 0f′(x)=0 at every point of that interval. Then f−1f^{-1}f−1 is differentiable and

(f−1)′(y)=1f′(f−1(y)).\bigl(f^{-1}\bigr)'(y) = \frac{1}{f'\bigl(f^{-1}(y)\bigr)}.(f−1)′(y)=f′(f−1(y))1​.

The condition f′(x)≠0f'(x) \neq 0f′(x)=0 is essential: it ensures that the graph of fff has no horizontal tangent, which would become a vertical tangent on the reflected graph of f−1f^{-1}f−1, and vertical tangents correspond to non-differentiable points as we saw in section 5.4. The geometric reading of the rule is equally direct: the slope of f−1f^{-1}f−1 at a point y0y_0y0​ is the reciprocal of the slope of fff at the corresponding point x0=f−1(y0)x_0 = f^{-1}(y_0)x0​=f−1(y0​). Reflection about y=xy = xy=x swaps rise and run, which is exactly what taking a reciprocal does to a slope.

What is the derivative of the natural logarithm?¶

We apply the rule to f(x)=exf(x) = e^xf(x)=ex, whose inverse is f−1(y)=ln⁡yf^{-1}(y) = \ln yf−1(y)=lny. From section 6.1 we know f′(x)=exf'(x) = e^xf′(x)=ex, so f′(f−1(y))=eln⁡y=yf'(f^{-1}(y)) = e^{\ln y} = yf′(f−1(y))=elny=y. The inverse function rule gives immediately:

(ln⁡y)′=1y,y>0.(\ln y)' = \frac{1}{y}, \quad y > 0.(lny)′=y1​,y>0.

Writing xxx as the standard variable name:

(ln⁡x)′=1x,x>0.(\ln x)' = \frac{1}{x}, \quad x > 0.(lnx)′=x1​,x>0.

This confirms the entry in the reference table of section 6.1 and closes the last open item in that table. The restriction x>0x > 0x>0 reflects the domain of the natural logarithm, which we established in section 4.3.

Returning to the damped oscillator: in section 6.3 we asked at what time the amplitude envelope A e−δtA\,e^{-\delta t}Ae−δt drops to a prescribed fraction α\alphaα of the initial displacement AAA. Solving e−δt=αe^{-\delta t} = \alphae−δt=α gives t=−ln⁡(α)/δt = -\ln(\alpha)/\deltat=−ln(α)/δ. If we now ask how sensitive this time is to a small change in the target fraction α\alphaα, we differentiate with respect to α\alphaα:

dtdα=−1δ α.\frac{dt}{d\alpha} = -\frac{1}{\delta\,\alpha}.dαdt​=−δα1​.

For our concrete system with δ=0.5 s−1\delta = 0.5~\text{s}^{-1}δ=0.5 s−1 and a target of α=0.5\alpha = 0.5α=0.5 (half the initial amplitude), this gives dt/dα=−1/(0.5⋅0.5)=−4 sdt/d\alpha = -1/(0.5 \cdot 0.5) = -4~\text{s}dt/dα=−1/(0.5⋅0.5)=−4 s. Each additional percentage point added to the target fraction shortens the settling time by approximately 0.04 s0.04~\text{s}0.04 s, a sensitivity figure that can inform a design decision.

What are the inverse trigonometric functions?¶

In section 4.4 we noted that the sine and cosine functions are not invertible over their full domains because they are periodic and therefore fail the horizontal line test. The standard remedy, introduced in section 4.2, is to restrict the domain to an interval on which the function is strictly monotonic.

The arcsine arcsin⁡(x)\arcsin(x)arcsin(x) is the inverse of sin⁡\sinsin restricted to [−π/2, π/2][-\pi/2,\, \pi/2][−π/2,π/2]. Its domain is [−1,1][-1, 1][−1,1] and its range is [−π/2, π/2][-\pi/2,\, \pi/2][−π/2,π/2].

The arccosine arccos⁡(x)\arccos(x)arccos(x) is the inverse of cos⁡\coscos restricted to [0,π][0, \pi][0,π]. Its domain is [−1,1][-1, 1][−1,1] and its range is [0,π][0, \pi][0,π].

The arctangent arctan⁡(x)\arctan(x)arctan(x) is the inverse of tan⁡\tantan restricted to (−π/2, π/2)(-\pi/2,\, \pi/2)(−π/2,π/2). Its domain is R\mathbb{R}R and its range is (−π/2, π/2)(-\pi/2,\, \pi/2)(−π/2,π/2).

What are the derivatives of the inverse trigonometric functions?¶

Summary and outlook¶

This section completes chapter 6. Starting from the chain rule applied to the identity f(f−1(y))=yf(f^{-1}(y)) = yf(f−1(y))=y, we derived the inverse function rule in three lines and used it to obtain the derivatives of ln⁡x\ln xlnx, arcsin⁡x\arcsin xarcsinx, arccos⁡x\arccos xarccosx, and arctan⁡x\arctan xarctanx. The logarithm derivative closes the last open entry in the reference table of section 6.1. The three inverse trigonometric derivatives each relied on the Pythagorean identity from section 4.5, demonstrating once more that the trigonometric identities built in chapter 4 are not background material but active tools in differentiation.

Together, chapters 5 and 6 tell a single story. Chapter 5 defined the derivative rigorously through limits and computed it for polynomials by direct calculation. Chapter 6 replaced that direct calculation with five rules: the power rule, the factor and sum rules, the product and quotient rules, the chain rule, and the inverse function rule. Every function built from the elementary families of chapters 3 and 4 is now differentiable in a finite number of steps, without returning to the limit definition. These rules are the foundation on which the applications of differential calculus rest: finding the velocity and acceleration of the damped oscillator, locating the maxima and minima of engineering functions, and constructing the linear and polynomial approximations that allow nonlinear problems to be handled with linear methods.
