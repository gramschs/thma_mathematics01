7.1 Higher-Order Derivatives

The differentiation rules assembled in chapter 6 give us a systematic way to compute the derivative f′f'f′ of any combination of elementary functions. That derivative is itself a function, defined on the same domain, and there is nothing preventing us from applying the same rules to it again. Repeating the process produces the higher-order derivatives, each one encoding a new layer of information about the original function: not just how fast fff is changing, but how that rate of change is itself evolving, and then how that evolution is evolving in turn.

Learning goals

  • You know the definition of the second derivative f′′(x)=(f′(x))′f''(x) = \bigl(f'(x)\bigr)'f′′(x)=(f′(x))′ and of the nnn-th derivative f(n)(x)f^{(n)}(x)f(n)(x).

  • You are familiar with the notations f′′(x)f''(x)f′′(x), d2fdx2\dfrac{d^2f}{dx^2}dx2d2f​, and f(n)(x)f^{(n)}(x)f(n)(x).

  • You can compute higher-order derivatives of polynomials, exponential functions, and trigonometric functions.

What happens when we differentiate the derivative?¶

In chapter 5 we derived the derivative function f′(x)=0.04x−0.6f'(x) = 0.04x - 0.6f′(x)=0.04x−0.6 for the parabolic marble track f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6f(x) = 0.02x^2 - 0.6x + 6f(x)=0.02x2−0.6x+6. Applying the power rule from chapter 6 one more time:

(f′(x))′=(0.04x−0.6)′=0.04.\bigl(f'(x)\bigr)' = (0.04x - 0.6)' = 0.04.(f′(x))′=(0.04x−0.6)′=0.04.

The result is a positive constant. It tells us that the slope of the marble track increases at a steady rate as xxx grows: for every centimetre of horizontal travel the slope rises by exactly 0.04. The track curves upward uniformly from one end to the other, which is exactly what we expect from a parabola.

This new function is called the second derivative of fff, written f′′(x)f''(x)f′′(x). It is the derivative of the derivative, obtained by applying the differentiation process twice. We do not have to stop there: the second derivative is itself a function, and we can differentiate it a third time to obtain the third derivative f′′′(x)f'''(x)f′′′(x), a fourth time to obtain f(4)(x)f^{(4)}(x)f(4)(x), and so on. For the marble track the third derivative is f′′′(x)=(0.04)′=0f'''(x) = (0.04)' = 0f′′′(x)=(0.04)′=0: the second derivative is constant, and the derivative of a constant is zero. All derivatives beyond the second vanish for this quadratic.

What is the nnn-th derivative?¶

Applying the differentiation rules repeatedly produces a sequence of functions. Writing f(0)=ff^{(0)} = ff(0)=f for the original function and f(1)=f′f^{(1)} = f'f(1)=f′ for the first derivative, each subsequent member of the sequence is defined by one further differentiation:

f(n)(x)=(f(n−1)(x))′.f^{(n)}(x) = \Bigl(f^{(n-1)}(x)\Bigr)'.f(n)(x)=(f(n−1)(x))′.
What is ... the nnn-th derivative?

Let fff be a function that can be differentiated nnn times. The nnn-th derivative of fff is the function f(n)f^{(n)}f(n) obtained by applying the differentiation operator nnn times in succession:

f(n)(x)=(⋯(f′(x))′⋯ )′⏟n times.f^{(n)}(x) = \underbrace{\Bigl(\cdots\bigl(f'(x)\bigr)'\cdots\Bigr)'}_{\text{$n$ times}}.f(n)(x)=n times(⋯(f′(x))′⋯)′​​.

We write f′=f(1)f' = f^{(1)}f′=f(1), f′′=f(2)f'' = f^{(2)}f′′=f(2), f′′′=f(3)f''' = f^{(3)}f′′′=f(3), and use the superscript notation f(n)f^{(n)}f(n) for n≥4n \geq 4n≥4.

The Leibniz notation for higher derivatives extends the first-derivative symbol dfdx\dfrac{df}{dx}dxdf​ in a natural direction:

f′′(x)=d2fdx2,f′′′(x)=d3fdx3,f(n)(x)=dnfdxn.f''(x) = \frac{d^2 f}{dx^2}, \qquad f'''(x) = \frac{d^3 f}{dx^3}, \qquad f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d^n f}{dx^n}.f′′(x)=dx2d2f​,f′′′(x)=dx3d3f​,f(n)(x)=dxndnf​.

The superscript on ddd counts how many times we differentiate, and the matching superscript on dxdxdx keeps the dimensional bookkeeping consistent. Both families of notation are standard in engineering literature.

How do we compute the higher derivatives for a roller coaster lift hill?¶

We now introduce the running example for this chapter: a section of a roller coaster lift hill. The track carries passengers from the boarding platform at ground level up to the summit of the first drop. We place the origin at the base of the lift and measure height hhh and horizontal distance xxx both in metres. The shape of the track is modelled by the cubic polynomial

h(x)=130 x2−12700 x3,x∈[0,60].h(x) = \frac{1}{30}\,x^2 - \frac{1}{2700}\,x^3, \quad x \in [0, 60].h(x)=301​x2−27001​x3,x∈[0,60].

Two boundary conditions are built into this formula. At the boarding platform, h(0)=0h(0) = 0h(0)=0: the track starts at ground level. At the summit,

h(60)=360030−2160002700=120−80=40 m.h(60) = \frac{3600}{30} - \frac{216000}{2700} = 120 - 80 = 40~\text{m}.h(60)=303600​−2700216000​=120−80=40 m.

The lift hill raises passengers 40 metres over a horizontal span of 60 metres.

We now apply the power rule and constant-multiple rule from chapter 6 to compute the successive derivatives. The first derivative gives the gradient, that is, the slope of the track at each horizontal position:

h′(x)=x15−x2900=x(60−x)900.h'(x) = \frac{x}{15} - \frac{x^2}{900} = \frac{x(60 - x)}{900}.h′(x)=15x​−900x2​=900x(60−x)​.

The factor (60−x)(60 - x)(60−x) confirms that h′(0)=0h'(0) = 0h′(0)=0 and h′(60)=0h'(60) = 0h′(60)=0: the track starts horizontally at the boarding platform and levels off again at the summit. In between, the slope is positive everywhere. Its maximum value is h′(30)=30⋅30900=1h'(30) = \frac{30 \cdot 30}{900} = 1h′(30)=90030⋅30​=1, reached at the midpoint x=30 mx = 30~\text{m}x=30 m, where the track inclines at 45°45°45°.

The second derivative measures how quickly the gradient is changing:

h′′(x)=115−x450=30−x450.h''(x) = \frac{1}{15} - \frac{x}{450} = \frac{30 - x}{450}.h′′(x)=151​−450x​=45030−x​.

At x=0x = 0x=0 we have h′′(0)=30450=115>0h''(0) = \frac{30}{450} = \frac{1}{15} > 0h′′(0)=45030​=151​>0: the slope is increasing. At x=30x = 30x=30, h′′(30)=0h''(30) = 0h′′(30)=0: the slope has stopped growing. At x=60x = 60x=60, h′′(60)=−115<0h''(60) = -\frac{1}{15} < 0h′′(60)=−151​<0: the slope is now decreasing back toward zero. The sign change of h′′h''h′′ marks a qualitative shift in the geometry of the track that we will examine carefully in sections 7.3 and 7.4.

The third derivative is constant:

h′′′(x)=−1450.h'''(x) = -\frac{1}{450}.h′′′(x)=−4501​.

One further differentiation gives h(4)(x)=0h^{(4)}(x) = 0h(4)(x)=0, and all higher derivatives are zero. This is expected: hhh is a polynomial of degree three, and differentiating three times reduces it to a nonzero constant.

How do higher-order derivatives behave for other function families?¶

For polynomials the pattern is systematic. Each differentiation reduces the degree by one, and after nnn differentiations of a degree-nnn polynomial we reach a nonzero constant. One step further gives zero.

The exponential function exe^xex behaves very differently. Its derivative is itself:

(ex)′=ex,(ex)′′=ex,…,(ex)(n)=ex.(e^x)' = e^x, \quad (e^x)'' = e^x, \quad \ldots, \quad (e^x)^{(n)} = e^x.(ex)′=ex,(ex)′′=ex,…,(ex)(n)=ex.

No matter how many times we differentiate, exe^xex remains unchanged. This remarkable stability is unique to the exponential and lies at the heart of why exe^xex solves a wide class of differential equations, which will become central in later courses on dynamics and systems theory.

The trigonometric functions form a four-step cycle. Starting from sin⁡x\sin xsinx:

(sin⁡x)′=cos⁡x,(cos⁡x)′=−sin⁡x,(−sin⁡x)′=−cos⁡x,(−cos⁡x)′=sin⁡x.(\sin x)' = \cos x, \quad (\cos x)' = -\sin x, \quad (-\sin x)' = -\cos x, \quad (-\cos x)' = \sin x.(sinx)′=cosx,(cosx)′=−sinx,(−sinx)′=−cosx,(−cosx)′=sinx.

After four differentiations we return to sin⁡x\sin xsinx, so (sin⁡x)(4)=sin⁡x(\sin x)^{(4)} = \sin x(sinx)(4)=sinx. The same four-step cycle holds for cos⁡x\cos xcosx, shifted by one step.

Summary and outlook¶

We have defined the nnn-th derivative by repeated application of the differentiation rules of chapter 6, and we have established the standard notations. For the roller coaster height profile h(x)=130x2−12700x3h(x) = \frac{1}{30}x^2 - \frac{1}{2700}x^3h(x)=301​x2−27001​x3, the first derivative describes the track gradient, the second derivative describes how quickly that gradient is changing, and the third derivative is a negative constant that reflects the cubic structure of the polynomial. The exponential function and the trigonometric functions show that higher derivatives need not simplify or vanish: they can remain constant or cycle indefinitely.

In the next section we turn to the direct physical meaning of these successive derivatives when the independent variable is time. A position function, its first derivative, and its second derivative tell the story of position, velocity, and acceleration. The third derivative has a name of its own, and an engineering significance that every designer of moving systems needs to know.
