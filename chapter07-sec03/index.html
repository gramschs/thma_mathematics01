7.3 Convexity and Concavity

In section 5.3 we saw that the sign of the first derivative tells us whether a function is increasing or decreasing: f′(x)>0f'(x) > 0f′(x)>0 means the graph rises, f′(x)<0f'(x) < 0f′(x)<0 means it falls. The second derivative provides one more layer of information. It tells us not just whether the function is rising or falling, but whether the rate of change is itself growing or shrinking. Geometrically, this is the difference between a curve that bends upward, like the base of a valley, and a curve that bends downward, like the crown of a hill. This bending character shapes not only the appearance of the graph but the relationship between the curve and its tangent lines.

Learning goals

  • You know the definitions of convexity (concave up) and concavity (concave down) of a function on an interval.

  • You can determine intervals of convexity and concavity from the sign of the second derivative: f′′(x)>0f''(x) > 0f′′(x)>0 implies convex, f′′(x)<0f''(x) < 0f′′(x)<0 implies concave.

  • You understand the geometric interpretation of the second derivative’s sign in terms of the curvature of the graph and the behavior of the tangent line.

What are convexity and concavity?¶

The two shapes we have described have precise names. A function whose slope is increasing, so that the graph bends upward, is called convex on that interval, or equivalently concave up. A function whose slope is decreasing, so that the graph bends downward, is called concave on that interval, or equivalently concave down.

What is ... convexity and concavity?

Let fff be twice differentiable on an open interval III.

fff is convex (concave up) on III if f′′(x)>0f''(x) > 0f′′(x)>0 for all x∈Ix \in Ix∈I. The slope is increasing, the graph bends upward, and every tangent line lies below the graph on that interval.

fff is concave (concave down) on III if f′′(x)<0f''(x) < 0f′′(x)<0 for all x∈Ix \in Ix∈I. The slope is decreasing, the graph bends downward, and every tangent line lies above the graph on that interval.

The tangent-line characterisation in the definition deserves attention. For a convex function the tangent at any point underestimates the function value at nearby points: the true curve is always above the straight-line approximation. For a concave function the tangent overestimates: the true curve is below. This connects directly to the linear approximation formula f(x)≈f(x0)+f′(x0)(x−x0)f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0)f(x)≈f(x0​)+f′(x0​)(x−x0​) developed in section 5.5. On a convex interval the formula underestimates the true value, and on a concave interval it overestimates it.

Where does convexity matter in engineering?¶

Convexity and concavity appear throughout structural and civil engineering wherever the shape of a loaded member or a geometric path must be described precisely.

In beam theory, the deflection of a simply supported beam under load satisfies the relation EI y′′=M(x)EI\,y'' = M(x)EIy′′=M(x), where EEE is the modulus of elasticity, III is the second moment of area, and M(x)M(x)M(x) is the bending moment at cross-section xxx. The sign of y′′y''y′′ at each section identifies whether the beam is sagging (convex upward, y′′>0y'' > 0y′′>0) or hogging (concave, y′′<0y'' < 0y′′<0). These two regimes produce tension in opposite fibres of the cross-section, and the reinforcement layout in a concrete beam is designed with this distinction in mind.

In road and railway design, the vertical alignment alternates between convex sections at summits and concave sections in valleys. The curvature at each point, directly proportional to ∣h′′∣|h''|∣h′′∣, determines the minimum sight distance on a crest curve and the dynamic wheel load on a vehicle passing through a sag curve at speed. Both quantities are checked against design standards before a vertical profile is approved.

Summary and outlook¶

We have given the sign of the second derivative a geometric interpretation. A positive second derivative means the slope is growing and the graph bends upward: the function is convex. A negative second derivative means the slope is shrinking and the graph bends downward: the function is concave. For the roller coaster height profile the lower half of the lift hill is convex and the upper half is concave.

At x=30 mx = 30~\text{m}x=30 m, where h′′=0h'' = 0h′′=0, the curvature character reverses. This transition is not just a zero of the second derivative: it marks a point where the function changes from one geometric type to another. Such a point has its own name, and confirming that it is genuine requires a check that goes beyond simply setting h′′=0h'' = 0h′′=0. That is the subject of the next section.
