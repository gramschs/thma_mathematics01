7.4 Inflection Points

A function can be convex on one interval and concave on another, as we saw in section 7.3 for the roller coaster height profile. The point where the curve transitions from one type to the other marks a qualitative change in shape: the bending reverses direction. On the lift hill, this transition occurs at x=30 mx = 30~\text{m}x=30 m, where the track changes from curving upward like the base of a bowl to curving downward like the crown of a dome. Points of this kind appear throughout engineering, from the bending moment diagrams of loaded beams to the transition curves of roads and railways, and identifying them precisely requires a careful argument.

Learning goals

  • You know the definition of an inflection point as a point where the sign of the second derivative (and thus the convexity/concavity of the function) changes.

  • You can identify candidates for inflection points using the necessary condition f′′(x0)=0f''(x_0) = 0f′′(x0​)=0.

What is an inflection point?¶

What does the inflection point look like on the graph?¶

The inflection at x=30x = 30x=30 is visible in the geometry of the lift hill profile. On the lower, convex half the tangent line at any point lies below the curve nearby: the curve climbs above its own linear approximation. On the upper, concave half the tangent line lies above the curve: the curve falls short of its linear approximation. At the inflection point itself the tangent line crosses from one side of the curve to the other, momentarily coinciding in both value and slope with the track but transitioning from an underestimate to an overestimate.

This crossing is a clear visual sign of an inflection point. For the marble track studied in chapters 5 and 6, no such crossing occurs because f′′(x)=0.04>0f''(x) = 0.04 > 0f′′(x)=0.04>0 everywhere: the parabola is convex on its entire domain, and its tangent lines never cross the curve.

The location of the inflection point is also important for the passengers. On the lower half, the track pushes upward against the car with a centripetal force that adds to the apparent weight, pressing passengers into their seats. On the upper half, the track curves in the opposite sense, and the centripetal force direction reverses, reducing the apparent weight. The inflection point at x=30x = 30x=30 m marks the boundary between these two experiences.

Where do inflection points appear in engineering?¶

Inflection points arise wherever a quantity transitions between two opposite curvature regimes, and they serve as natural reference points in design and analysis.

In beam theory, the deflection curve y(x)y(x)y(x) of a beam satisfies EI y′′=M(x)EI\,y'' = M(x)EIy′′=M(x), where M(x)M(x)M(x) is the bending moment. Wherever M(x)=0M(x) = 0M(x)=0, the second derivative of the deflection vanishes, and if MMM changes sign there, the deflection curve has an inflection point. Engineers call this position the point of contraflexure. It separates the region where the beam sags (convex upward) from the region where it hogs (concave), and it determines where to place construction joints and how to arrange reinforcement in concrete structures.

In road and railway design, every transition from a sag curve to a crest curve contains an inflection point in the vertical profile. The curvature is zero at that point, and design standards specify the minimum length of the transition zone around it to ensure that the change in centripetal force imposed on vehicles remains within comfortable bounds. The mathematics behind those standards uses exactly the analysis of higher-order derivatives that we have developed in this chapter.

In the analysis of control systems, a sigmoid-shaped step response has exactly one inflection point, corresponding to the moment of maximum rate of change in the output. Identifying this point, and measuring the slope of the response curve there, is a standard method for estimating the time constants of the underlying system model.

Summary and outlook¶

We have defined an inflection point as a point where the sign of the second derivative, and therefore the convexity/concavity of a function, changes. The condition f′′(x0)=0f''(x_0) = 0f′′(x0​)=0 is necessary but not sufficient; the example g(x)=x4g(x) = x^4g(x)=x4 shows that we must also check whether f′′f''f′′ changes sign at x0x_0x0​ to confirm an inflection point.

For the roller coaster height profile h(x)h(x)h(x), the unique inflection at x=30 mx = 30~\text{m}x=30 m marks the transition from the bowl-shaped lower half of the lift hill (convex) to the dome-shaped upper half (concave), at mid-height and at the point of maximum gradient. In the next chapter we will use the same idea—linking the sign of f′′f''f′′ to the local shape of the graph—to classify stationary points f′(x0)=0f'(x_0) = 0f′(x0​)=0 as local minima, local maxima, or neither via the second derivative test.
