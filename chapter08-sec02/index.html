8.2 Curve Sketching

We now have all the tools needed to draw a qualitatively accurate graph of any differentiable function without plotting individual points. The zeros of fff anchor the curve to the axis. The sign of f′f'f′ marks the intervals of increase and decrease identified in chapter 5, and its zeros locate the critical points classified in section 8.1. The sign of f′′f''f′′ marks the convex and concave intervals from section 7.3, and its zeros locate the inflection points of section 7.4. Combining all this information into a single coherent sketch is the technique of curve sketching, and it brings together nearly everything we have developed since chapter 5.

Learning goals

  • You can carry out a systematic curve sketching procedure: determining the domain, zeros, yyy-intercept, monotonicity intervals, local extrema, convexity intervals, and inflection points.

  • You can produce a qualitatively correct sketch of a differentiable function using only the information obtained from fff, f′f'f′, and f′′f''f′′.

  • You understand the relationships between the graphs of fff, f′f'f′, and f′′f''f′′, and you can read information about one from the graph of another.

What is the curve sketching procedure?¶

Every curve sketch follows the same sequence of steps, each building on the last:

  1. Determine the domain and note any restrictions, asymptotes, or symmetries.

  2. Find the yyy-intercept f(0)f(0)f(0) and the zeros of fff.

  3. Compute f′f'f′, find the critical points where f′=0f' = 0f′=0, and determine the sign of f′f'f′ on each subinterval.

  4. Classify each critical point as a local maximum or minimum using the second derivative test from section 8.1.

  5. Compute f′′f''f′′, find the inflection candidates where f′′=0f'' = 0f′′=0, and verify sign changes to confirm each one.

  6. Evaluate fff at all critical points, inflection points, and domain endpoints.

  7. Combine all findings into a coherent sketch.

We now carry out this procedure for a concrete engineering function, then examine what the three graphs fff, f′f'f′, and f′′f''f′′ reveal about each other.

How do we sketch the speed profile of a test sled?¶

A motorised sled on a straight test track performs a controlled braking sequence. Starting from rest, the sled accelerates to a test speed, then brakes back to a complete stop before re-accelerating. The speed of the sled, in m/s, as a function of time ttt in seconds is modelled by

How do the graphs of vvv, v′v'v′, and v′′v''v′′ relate to each other?¶

The three functions vvv, v′v'v′, and v′′v''v′′ are not independent: each is the derivative of the one above it. Every feature of vvv is encoded in the sign or zero of v′v'v′ and v′′v''v′′, and reading across the three graphs fluently is one of the core skills this section develops.

Where vvv has a local maximum, v′v'v′ crosses zero from positive to negative. At t=1t = 1t=1, the graph of v′v'v′ descends through zero. Where vvv has a local minimum, v′v'v′ crosses zero from negative to positive. At t=3t = 3t=3, v′v'v′ ascends through zero. The zeros of v′v'v′ are exactly the local extrema of vvv.

Where vvv has an inflection point, v′v'v′ has a local extremum. The inflection of vvv at t=2t = 2t=2 corresponds to a local minimum of v′v'v′: v′(2)=3(1)(−1)=−3v'(2) = 3(1)(-1) = -3v′(2)=3(1)(−1)=−3, the most negative value of v′v'v′ on the interval. This is consistent with the geometric interpretation: at the inflection the curve transitions from concave to convex, which means the slope, already negative and decreasing, reaches its most negative point and then begins to recover.

The graph of v′′=6t−12v'' = 6t - 12v′′=6t−12 is a straight line that is negative to the left of t=2t = 2t=2 and positive to the right. It crosses zero at the inflection point of vvv, is negative at the local maximum t=1t = 1t=1 (confirming the downward curvature there), and positive at the local minimum t=3t = 3t=3 (confirming the upward curvature). All three graphs tell the same story from three different vantage points.

Where does curve sketching appear in engineering?¶

The ability to read qualitative behaviour directly from derivatives is valuable whenever a function is known in formula form and the engineer needs to understand its structure quickly, without numerical simulation.

In control engineering, the step response of a system is a function of time whose zeros (crossing times), local extrema (overshoot values), and inflection points (moment of maximum rate of rise) each have direct engineering significance. An engineer sketches the response curve from the transfer function in order to check whether the system will produce acceptable behaviour before committing to a detailed simulation. In thermodynamics, the efficiency of a cycle as a function of one operating parameter can be sketched to identify the optimal operating region and the sensitivity of the efficiency to changes away from the optimum.

Summary and outlook¶

We have assembled the full curve sketching procedure into a seven-step sequence and applied it to the speed profile v(t)=t(t−3)2v(t) = t(t - 3)^2v(t)=t(t−3)2. The sketch required no numerical plotting: the zeros, critical points, inflection point, and curvature sign were all determined from v′v'v′ and v′′v''v′′ alone. The three-panel view of vvv, v′v'v′, and v′′v''v′′ showed how each feature of the curve is encoded in the sign or zero of its derivatives.

Curve sketching describes a function that is already given to us. In engineering the more common task is the reverse: we have a design goal and must find the variable values that achieve the best outcome. Setting up and solving such problems is the subject of the next section.
